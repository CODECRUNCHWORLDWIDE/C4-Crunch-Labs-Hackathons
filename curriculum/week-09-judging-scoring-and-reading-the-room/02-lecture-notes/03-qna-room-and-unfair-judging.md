# Lecture 3 — Q&A, Reading the Room, and Unfair Judging

> **Duration:** ~1.5 hours of reading.
> **Outcome:** You can anticipate the five most-asked questions for your build, answer each in <30 seconds, deploy the bridge sentence on a hard question and the "I don't know" sentence when honest, read the audience attention curve and the four named body-language cues in the room, deploy the named recovery move per cue, and respond to judging that feels unfair using the C4 voice on what is fair to push back on. You can also play the *other* role — be a judge yourself — using the first-time-judging sidebar.

If you only remember one sentence from this lecture, remember this:

> **The pitch is half the slot. The Q&A is the other half. The team that rehearses only the pitch rehearses only half of what the judge will score. Rehearse both.**

Lecture 2 covered the 3-minute pitch. This lecture covers everything that happens *around* the pitch — the Q&A after, the room-reading during, the response when judging feels unfair after, and the be-a-judge sidebar when the team is invited to flip seats later in the cohort.

This lecture has four parts. Part 1 (section 2) covers reading the room — the audience attention curve, the four named judge cues, and the named recovery moves. Part 2 (section 3) covers the Q&A drill — the five-question anticipation, the 30-second cap, the bridge sentence, the "I don't know" pattern. Part 3 (section 4) covers unfair judging — the C4 voice on what is fair to push back on, the result-review-request channel, and the boundary. Part 4 (section 5) is the be-a-judge sidebar. Section 6 is the recap.

## 1. Why the surrounding minutes matter

The judging slot is not 3 minutes; it is *typically 4-7 minutes*. The 3-minute pitch is the team's prepared content. The 1-4 surrounding minutes are:

- **The opening 15-30 seconds** before the team starts the pitch — handshake, introductions, the judge starting the clipboard.
- **The Q&A after the pitch** — typically 60-180 seconds, depending on the format.
- **The closing 15-30 seconds** — the judge thanks the team, makes a note, and moves to the next table or panel slot.

The surrounding minutes are where the *room-reading* and the *Q&A handling* happen. The team that has rehearsed only the 3-minute pitch is unprepared for 25-50% of the judging slot. The team that has rehearsed both is prepared for the whole window.

> **C4 voice:** the pitch is the *prepared* content; the Q&A is the *unprepared-looking* content. Both can be rehearsed; both should be. The team that rehearses only the pitch reveals the gap in the Q&A; the team that rehearses both delivers the same level of polish across the entire slot.

## 2. Reading the room

The team can read the room. The room is not opaque. The judge's body, the judge's eyes, the judge's clipboard, the judge's posture — all of them broadcast signals the team can read in real time.

### 2.1 The audience attention curve

Across the 3-minute pitch, a single judge's attention follows a predictable U-curve:

```
ATTENTION (high) │
                 │ ___                        ___
                 │/   \                      /   \
                 │     \                    /
                 │      \                  /
                 │       \________________/
                 │
                 └─────────────────────────────── TIME
                 0:00   0:30        1:30        3:00
                 Opening   Demo middle  Closing
                 spike    attention dip recovery
```

Three regions:

- **The opening spike (0:00 — 0:30).** Attention is highest. The judge's clipboard is in hand; the judge is forming the first impression. The problem beat lands here.
- **The middle dip (0:30 — 1:30 ish).** Attention drifts. The judge has formed the first impression; they are now waiting for the *demonstrating* portion to give them rubric data points. If the demo is not landing, attention dips further.
- **The closing recovery (1:30 — 3:00).** Attention rises back. The judge knows the slot is ending; they are forming the *closing impression* and getting ready to score.

The team's job: keep attention high during the dip. The 30-90-45-15 arc puts the demo at 0:30-2:00 specifically to hold attention across the dip. The team that holds attention across the dip lands the demo cleanly and gives the judge clean rubric data points.

### 2.2 The four named judge body-language cues

Each cue maps to a specific inflection point on the curve. Each cue has a *named recovery move*.

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  THE FOUR JUDGE CUES                                                │
│                                                                    │
│  Cue 1: Clipboard pickup  → Judge starting to score; team should   │
│                              re-anchor on rubric-relevant detail   │
│  Cue 2: Eye-contact loss   → Attention drifting; slow cadence and  │
│                              return eye contact                     │
│  Cue 3: Lean-back          → Judge mentally closing; pivot to the  │
│                              ask early                              │
│  Cue 4: Side-glance        → Judge consulting other judges or note;│
│                              name the elephant in the next sentence│
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### 2.3 Cue 1 — Clipboard pickup

Signal: the judge picks up their rubric clipboard (or opens their rubric tablet, or starts writing on paper). Typically occurs at:

- **0:15-0:30 if the team's problem framing landed.** The judge is scoring originality on the user-pain framing.
- **0:30 if the team transitioned cleanly to the demo.** The judge is starting to score design/polish.
- **2:00-2:15 if the tech beat is landing.** The judge is scoring technical complexity.

What this cue means: the judge is *actively scoring*, which is good. The team should give the judge clean rubric-relevant detail to score.

Recovery move (when the cue appears): **re-anchor on rubric-relevant detail in the next sentence.** If the team was about to add an unrelated tangent, drop it. Stay on the rubric-relevant content.

Example: at 1:45, the team is in the demo beat. The judge picks up the clipboard. The team's *planned* next sentence was "and one thing I think is fun is the easter egg we built." The team's *recovered* next sentence is "and the originality lever here is that no other tool we found does this for [specific user]." The cue redirected the script in real time.

### 2.4 Cue 2 — Eye-contact loss

Signal: the judge looks away from the team or the screen. Drifts to their phone, their water bottle, the next team's table.

Typically occurs at:

- **0:50-1:20** if the demo is not landing (the team is talking but the screen has not changed meaningfully).
- **2:10-2:30** if the tech beat is too dense or too generic.
- **2:50** if the closing beat is overrunning.

What this cue means: attention is drifting *now*. The next 5-10 seconds need to recover it or the judge's rubric data points stop forming.

Recovery move: **slow the cadence and return eye contact.** Specifically:
1. Pause for one beat (~1 second).
2. Look directly at the judge.
3. Deliver the next sentence at 80% normal speed with clear eye contact.

The pause and the slowed cadence break the team's auto-pilot and pull the judge's attention back. Most judges who lost eye contact will return it when the team pauses; the human-attention reflex is reliable.

### 2.5 Cue 3 — Lean-back

Signal: the judge leans backward in their chair, crosses their arms, or moves their hand away from the rubric.

Typically occurs at:

- **2:15-2:45** if the pitch is over-running or the tech beat is uninteresting.
- **Mid-Q&A** if the team's answer is rambling.

What this cue means: the judge is mentally closing the slot. They are no longer adding new data points to the rubric; they are now waiting to score and move on.

Recovery move: **pivot to the ask early.** If the team is at 2:20 and the judge leans back, abbreviate the tech beat and move to the 15-second ask. A clean 25-second close beats a rambling 45-second close.

In the Q&A, the recovery is the *single-sentence answer*. Stop expanding; stop adding caveats; finish the answer in one sentence and let the judge ask a follow-up if they want one.

### 2.6 Cue 4 — Side-glance

Signal: the judge looks at another judge (in a panel format), at their notes from a previous team, or at the room's facilitator.

Typically occurs at:

- **0:30-1:00** if the team's problem framing was unclear (the judge is consulting another judge on "did you follow that?").
- **2:00-2:30** if a technical claim is ambiguous (the judge is checking the tech with a co-judge).
- **Mid-Q&A** if the answer raised a new question the judge wants to coordinate on.

What this cue means: the judge has a question they have not yet asked. The question is brewing.

Recovery move: **name the elephant in the next sentence.** Acknowledge the ambiguous thing the judge is consulting about. Format:

```
"I notice the [thing] might be confusing — to be specific, [clarification].
We made this trade-off because [reasoning]."
```

Naming the ambiguous thing pre-empts the question the judge was going to ask. The team controls the framing; the judge does not have to interrupt to get the clarification.

### 2.7 What if the team misreads the cue

Reading the room is a probabilistic skill, not a deterministic one. The team will misread cues sometimes. Misreading is fine — the recovery moves are low-cost. Re-anchoring on rubric detail (cue 1's recovery) is good content regardless; slowing cadence (cue 2) is good delivery regardless; pivoting to the ask early (cue 3) is acceptable closing discipline; naming a potential ambiguity (cue 4) is a defensible move regardless of whether the judge was going to ask.

The team that *never* reads the room produces a pitch that is the same regardless of who is in front of them. The team that reads (even imperfectly) produces a pitch that adapts. The adapted pitch scores higher on presentation.

## 3. The Q&A drill

The Q&A is 60-180 seconds after the pitch. The team's job: answer concisely, accurately, and with the right tilt for the asker's mindset.

### 3.1 The five-question anticipation exercise

Every build has a *predictable* set of questions. The five most-asked questions cluster around five archetypes:

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  THE FIVE Q&A ARCHETYPES                                            │
│                                                                    │
│  Q1 — The user question                                             │
│  Q2 — The closest-competitor question                                │
│  Q3 — The technical question                                         │
│  Q4 — The follow-through question                                    │
│  Q5 — The challenge question                                         │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

For each archetype, the team writes the specific question for *their* build and a 30-second answer. The exercise is the *writing*; the rehearsal is the aloud delivery of the written answers.

### 3.2 Archetype Q1 — The user question

The investor-mindset judge's canonical opener. Variants:

```
"Who is the user, specifically?"
"How did you choose this user?"
"Have you talked to any users?"
"What does the user do today, without your build?"
```

The team's 30-second answer template:

```
"The user is [specific user]. We chose this user because [reason —
ideally a user-research signal, even a small one]. Today, this user
does [current alternative], which has [specific pain we are
solving]. We talked to [N] users during/before the build, and the
consistent signal was [signal]."
```

Five sentences. ~25-28 seconds. The answer mirrors the problem beat of the pitch (Lecture 2 §2.1) but adds the user-research depth.

### 3.3 Archetype Q2 — The closest-competitor question

Both mindsets ask this; investor-mindset more aggressively. Variants:

```
"What is the closest existing tool that does this?"
"Why hasn't [X company] built this already?"
"How is this different from [Y app]?"
```

The team's 30-second answer template:

```
"The closest existing tool we found is [name]. It does [thing] for
[user]. We differ in [specific feature/specific user/specific
context]. The reason [X company] has not built this is [hypothesis
— usually: market is too narrow for them, but we are sized right
for it; OR: technical decision they would not make for their main
product, but we can]."
```

The honest answer ("yes, we are aware of X; we differ by Y") is high-signal. The dishonest answer ("there is nothing like this") loses points immediately because the judge usually *can* think of one tool that is similar.

### 3.4 Archetype Q3 — The technical question

The engineer-mindset judge's canonical opener. Variants:

```
"What does the code path look like for [user action]?"
"How does the build handle [load condition]?"
"What was the hardest technical decision?"
"What would break under [load]?"
```

The team's 30-second answer template:

```
"The code path is [step 1] → [step 2] → [step 3]. The hardest part
was [hardest sub-problem]. We solved it by [solution]. The system
would break if [scale condition], and the next-step mitigation is
[mitigation we have thought about]."
```

The engineer-judge scores technical credibility on this answer. The team that names the *system's break point* (and the mitigation) scores higher than the team that claims "it would scale fine."

### 3.5 Archetype Q4 — The follow-through question

The investor-mindset judge's late-pitch question. Variants:

```
"Would you keep building this after the hackathon?"
"What is your plan for next week?"
"How serious are you about this?"
```

The team's 30-second answer template:

```
"We plan to [specific next step] in the next [specific time window].
The reason is [team's actual commitment — not aspirational]. The
team's role going forward is [who keeps building, who supports].
The first thing we will ship in the next [time window] is [specific
deliverable]."
```

The honest "we will ship X by Y" answer is high-signal. The aspirational "we hope to keep going" answer is low-signal because every team says it.

If the team genuinely does not plan to keep building (because it was a course project, because the team disperses after the event), say so. The honest "this was a learning project; the carry-out is the lesson, not the build" answer is also high-signal because it is rare.

### 3.6 Archetype Q5 — The challenge question

The skeptical-judge's question. Variants:

```
"Why should this win over [other teams' similar build]?"
"What is the weakest part of this?"
"What would you change if you started over?"
```

The team's 30-second answer template:

```
"The strongest version of the challenge here is [steelman of the
challenger's position]. Our response is [our position]. We agree
that [point of partial agreement] is a real concern. The reason we
made the choice we did was [specific reasoning, not generic
defense]."
```

The team that *steelmans* the challenger (states the challenge fairly before responding) scores higher than the team that dismisses it. Investor-judges score this answer for *judgment*; engineer-judges score it for *self-awareness*.

### 3.7 The 30-second answer cap

Each Q&A answer is capped at 30 seconds. The cap is the discipline.

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  THE 30-SECOND ANSWER STRUCTURE                                     │
│                                                                    │
│  0:00 — 0:05   The direct answer (one sentence)                     │
│  0:05 — 0:20   The supporting evidence or reasoning (2-3 sentences) │
│  0:20 — 0:30   The concrete close — a fact, a number, a commitment  │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

Three beats inside a 30-second answer. The direct-answer-first discipline mirrors the demo-first discipline of the pitch. The team that *answers* before *explaining* scores higher on presentation across both mindsets.

### 3.8 The bridge sentence

When a question lands that the team is not ready for, the *bridge sentence* pattern lets the team route the answer back to a stronger answer.

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  THE BRIDGE SENTENCE — FORMAT                                       │
│                                                                    │
│  "[Acknowledge the question briefly] — [bridge phrase] — [pivot to│
│   stronger answer]."                                                │
│                                                                    │
│  Bridge phrases (use one):                                          │
│    "what we focused on was..."                                      │
│    "the related question we did spend time on was..."               │
│    "the way we think about that family of problem is..."            │
│    "the specific case we built for is..."                           │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

Example:

```
Judge asks: "Did you consider GDPR compliance?"
Team response: "We did not build for GDPR specifically — the related
question we did spend time on was data minimisation in the user's
saved-items table; we only store the title and the URL of each
item, never the user's identifier, so the privacy story for the
build is mostly a *what-we-don't-store* story rather than a
compliance story."
```

The team acknowledged the question (did not pretend it was answered), bridged to a related answer the team had thought through, and produced a coherent response in ~25 seconds.

The bridge sentence is not the same as the *dodge*. The dodge is "we didn't think about that; let me tell you about something else." The bridge is "we did not think about exactly that; we thought about *this related thing* which is in the same family of problem." The judge can tell the difference; honest bridges score higher than dishonest dodges.

### 3.9 The "I don't know" pattern

Sometimes the team genuinely does not know the answer. The honest "I don't know" pattern:

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  THE "I DON'T KNOW" SENTENCE — FORMAT                               │
│                                                                    │
│  "I don't know.                                                     │
│   The closest answer I can give is [closest related answer].        │
│   If we had two more weeks, we would investigate [direction]."      │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

Three sentences. ~15 seconds. The honest "I don't know" with a follow-up direction scores higher than the bluff that the team can deliver smoothly.

Judges almost universally reward the honest "I don't know" because:
- It tells the judge the team has *self-awareness about their knowledge boundaries*.
- It signals the team would be *trustworthy* in a real engineering or business context.
- It is rare — most teams bluff; the team that does not stands out.

The pattern has limits. If the question is one the team *should* know the answer to (the closest competitor, the user, the basic architecture), "I don't know" reads as unpreparedness. Use the pattern when the question is genuinely outside the team's preparation window (an academic-research question, a regulatory question, a question about a specific user demographic the team did not target).

## 4. Unfair judging

Sometimes the judging is unfair. The C4 voice does not pretend this never happens.

### 4.1 What "unfair" means here

Unfair judging is, narrowly, any of:

- **The judge did not see the demo work** (because the demo failed and the judge did not give the team the screenshot-recovery 10 seconds).
- **The judge graded the team on a dimension not on the published rubric** (rare but happens, especially when a sponsor judge over-weights their company's stack preference).
- **The judge was visibly distracted during the team's slot** (phone, other conversation, leaving early).
- **The judging time was meaningfully shorter for the team than for the average team** (the table-walk format means some teams get 3 minutes and some get 4-5; small variance is normal, large variance is unfair).
- **The rubric was applied inconsistently across teams** (the team is told later, anecdotally, that another team got more lenient scoring on the same dimension).

What "unfair" is *not*:

- **The team did not win.** Most teams do not win. Not-winning is the default.
- **The judge did not seem to like the team.** Personal-fit is not the rubric; the rubric is.
- **The judge asked a hard question.** Hard questions are the Q&A, not unfairness.
- **The judge did not understand the technical complexity.** The team's job is to *make the judge understand*; if they didn't, that is the team's gap, not the judge's.

The four "not"s are the C4 voice on what is *not* a basis for escalation. Most "this was unfair" reactions are one of the four "not"s; the work is to read the situation honestly.

### 4.2 What to do in the moment

In the moment — during or immediately after the pitch — the team's job is to *continue* and *log*.

- **Continue.** Even if the demo failed, even if the judge was distracted, even if the pitch was cut short — finish the slot. The next slot or the next judge may go differently; getting upset in the moment locks the team out of recovery.
- **Log.** Write down what happened in your `JUDGE-ROOM-LOG.md` within 24 hours. The log is the evidence base if the team decides to escalate later. Time, judges (by role, not by name unless documented), specific incident, team's interpretation, the team's *alternative* interpretation.

### 4.3 The result review request channel

Most MLH-affiliated events publish a *result review request* channel. This is the official path for raising a judging concern. The C4 voice on how to use it:

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  RESULT REVIEW REQUEST — WHEN AND HOW                               │
│                                                                    │
│  WHEN to file:                                                       │
│    - A documented procedural irregularity (judge missed the team,  │
│      judge applied a non-published rubric dimension, judging time  │
│      was meaningfully shorter than other teams' slots)              │
│    - A documented technical failure on the event's side (the live  │
│      URL was unreachable from the judging room because the event's │
│      WiFi failed)                                                    │
│    - The team has logged the incident within 24 hours                │
│                                                                      │
│  WHEN NOT to file:                                                   │
│    - The team did not win and feels they should have                 │
│    - A judge asked a hard question and the team felt singled out    │
│    - The team's demo failed for a team-side reason (live-coding,    │
│      localhost)                                                      │
│                                                                      │
│  HOW to file:                                                        │
│    - Use the event's official channel (Discord ticket, Devpost     │
│      comment to organisers, MLH organizer-contact form)             │
│    - Open with the specific procedural irregularity in 1 sentence  │
│    - Attach the team's log entry (timestamped) as the evidence     │
│    - State the team's ask explicitly (review the scoring, not      │
│      "give us the win")                                              │
│    - Sign with the team name and 1-2 team members' contact         │
│                                                                      │
│  TONE: factual, brief, evidence-led. Not aggrieved.                  │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The result-review-request channel exists because organisers know judging is imperfect. The channel is for procedural irregularities, not for outcome disagreements. The team that files for procedural irregularity is using the channel correctly; the team that files because they didn't win is misusing it and the organisers will note that.

### 4.4 When the channel does not exist

Smaller events, school-run events, and one-off events often do not have a formal result review request channel. The team's options:

- **DM the event organiser** (the cohort coordinator, the MLH coach if MLH-affiliated, the event's published contact). Same format as a result review request, just delivered as a direct message instead of a ticket.
- **Email a written summary** to the organiser within 48 hours. The written summary creates a record even when no formal channel exists.
- **Drop it.** Some unfair-judging moments are not worth the channel. The C4 voice's heuristic: file if the team's *learning* from filing is more than the team's *learning* from dropping. If the team would learn the same lesson either way, drop.

### 4.5 The boundary — what not to do

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  WHAT NOT TO DO WHEN JUDGING FEELS UNFAIR                            │
│                                                                    │
│  - Do not confront the judge during or immediately after the slot. │
│    The judge has 20-80 teams to score; the moment is wrong.        │
│                                                                    │
│  - Do not post publicly on social media or Reddit naming the judge.│
│    The MLH CoC and most events' CoCs treat this as harassment.     │
│                                                                    │
│  - Do not lobby other judges to "intervene" with the team's        │
│    judge. Judging consensus is the organiser's job, not the        │
│    team's.                                                          │
│                                                                    │
│  - Do not refuse to shake the judge's hand or otherwise breach     │
│    basic event etiquette. The team's reputation outlives the        │
│    event.                                                            │
│                                                                    │
│  - Do not let one unfair judging slot consume the entire           │
│    post-event week. File once, log once, move on.                   │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The team's reputation in the cohort and in the broader hackathon community is built over many events. One unfair slot is one data point; how the team responds is many data points. The response is what compounds.

### 4.6 The lesson from unfair judging

The honest C4 voice on this: most teams will experience at least one judging moment that feels unfair. The lesson is not "the system is broken." The lesson is "the system is *imperfect under load*."

The team's preparation strategy:

- **Build resilience.** The follow-up issue from Week 8's retro carries forward; the team that has a habit of *carry-out work* after the event recovers from unfair judging faster than the team whose entire emotional investment was in the event-day outcome.
- **Diversify the metric.** The team's "did we win" question is one metric. The team's "did we ship a clean log, a clean retro, a follow-up issue, and a working build" is a different metric. The second metric is in the team's control; the first is not. Track both.
- **Network with the judges who were fair.** Even at events with one unfair judging moment, there are usually 3-5 other judges who were fair. The relationships with the fair judges outlast the irritation with the unfair one.

> **C4 voice on unfair judging:** the win is shipping. The judging is the measurement. The measurement is sometimes wrong. The shipping is not.

## 5. The sidebar — being a judge yourself

Sometime in Weeks 10-14, some C4 grads will be invited to judge the *next* cohort's Week 7-8 dry-runs. The cohort coordinator's pattern: the strongest Week 9 mock-pitch performers and the strongest Week 8 retros get the early invite.

The sidebar covers what to do when the invite comes.

### 5.1 The first-time-judging mindset

You are now the *runner*'s peer, not the runner. The team being judged has the same anxieties you had this week. Your job is to apply the rubric consistently across teams, not to coach the teams or to give your favourite team a higher score because they reminded you of yourself.

The shift:

- From "I want to score well on the rubric" → to "I want to apply the rubric consistently across teams."
- From "I want the judges to be fair to me" → to "I want to be fair to every team I judge."
- From "I want the judge to like the build" → to "I want the rubric to score the build."

The first-time-judging seat is a calibration moment. The first time you judge, you will overscore (your impulse is "be kind") or underscore (your impulse is "be tough") relative to the median. The cohort coordinator's review of your scoring shows you which direction you tilted.

### 5.2 The rubric pre-read

Before the judging slot, read the rubric *aloud once* with another judge. Hearing the rubric in your own voice anchors the dimensions and the scale.

If you are a sole judge for a small event, read the rubric aloud to yourself. The discipline matters; the audience is secondary.

Pre-read items:
- The five dimensions (or whatever the event's specific dimensions are).
- The 1-5 (or 1-10) scale's anchor descriptions.
- The weighting (if any).
- Any prize-track-specific overlay rubrics.

### 5.3 The table-walk rhythm

At a table-walk event, the judge rotates through team tables. Typical rhythm:

```
Per table: 4 minutes total
  0:00 — 0:30   Introduce yourself, ask the team to start
  0:30 — 3:00   Listen to the pitch
  3:00 — 3:45   Q&A — 1-2 questions
  3:45 — 4:00   Thank the team, score, move to next table
```

If the event runs 3 rotations of judges across 20 teams, each table sees a judge 3 times across the day. The judge's job is the same each slot: listen, score, move.

Discipline:
- **Stay on time.** A judge who runs over at table 7 cascades the day for tables 8-20. Use a phone timer.
- **Score immediately.** Write the score on the rubric within 60 seconds of leaving the table. Do not batch up "I'll score them all at the end." Memory degrades; consistency drops.
- **Do not double back.** If you realise at table 12 that table 4's design was actually better than you scored it, do not retroactively rescore. The scoring is anchored to the moment of judging; retroactive rescoring corrupts the consistency.

### 5.4 The panel rhythm

At a panel-format event, the team presents to a seated panel of 3-7 judges. Typical rhythm:

```
Per team: 7-10 minutes total
  0:00 — 3:00   Team's pitch
  3:00 — 6:00   Q&A — each panel judge asks 1 question
  6:00 — 7:00   Panel coordinates ("any other questions?")
  7:00 — 7:30   Score (each judge scores independently first)
  7:30 — 10:00  Brief panel consensus discussion (if time)
```

Panel judging discipline:
- **Take notes during the pitch and Q&A.** You cannot rely on memory across 8-15 teams over 4 hours.
- **Ask the question your seat asks.** If you are the technical judge on the panel, ask the technical question. Do not duplicate another judge's question.
- **Score independently before the consensus discussion.** The discussion can influence; the independent score is the unbiased anchor.
- **The consensus discussion is for *outliers*.** If a team is scored 4.2 by three judges and 2.1 by one judge, the outlier's reasoning matters. If all four judges scored within 0.5, no consensus discussion is needed.

### 5.5 The "do not coach" rule

Judges do not coach teams. Coaching is the cohort coordinator's job, the event's mentor program's job, the team's faculty advisor's job — not the judge's.

What "do not coach" means in practice:

- **Do not tell the team what they should have built.** The judging is for what they did build.
- **Do not tell the team how to improve their pitch mid-slot.** Note it for yourself, score accordingly, but do not lecture.
- **Do not promise the team anything ("come work at my company", "I'll connect you with X")** unless you are *also* the connector for your own organisation. Promises from judges that judges cannot keep are a known pattern of harm.
- **Do reply to a team's later DM asking for feedback**, *after* the event. Post-event feedback is welcomed by most teams and is not the same as mid-event coaching.

The "do not coach" rule protects the team (they get scored on what they did, not what they could have been told to do) and protects the judge (you do not become responsible for the team's outcome).

### 5.6 Scoring under fatigue

Judging 20-80 teams in 4 hours is fatiguing. The judge's fatigue follows a curve similar to the team's hackathon fatigue. Calibration:

- **The first 5 teams score harshly** because the judge has not yet calibrated to the day's median.
- **The middle 10-30 teams score on the median.**
- **The last 5-10 teams score leniently** because the judge has decision fatigue and defaults to 3 of 5 across dimensions.

Counter-measure:
- **Re-read your rubric every 5 teams.** The pre-read is at the start; the re-reads are the calibration tune-up.
- **Sip water; do not skip the break the organisers built in.** The break is in the schedule for a reason.
- **Spot-check your scoring distribution at the end.** If you scored 18 of 20 teams at 4 of 5 on polish, you compressed the scale. Note this for the consensus discussion.

### 5.7 The panel-versus-table-walk preference

Some judges prefer panel; some prefer table-walk. The patterns:

- **Panel** is better for teams whose pitch is rehearsed and whose Q&A is strong. The seated format gives the judges time to score deliberately.
- **Table-walk** is better for teams whose build is impressive but whose pitch is rough. The judges see the build up close; the rough pitch matters less because the build's evidence is right there.

As a judge, you may have preference; as the runner, the format is set by the event. The team's preparation does not change much across formats — the 30-90-45-15 arc works in both; the Q&A drill works in both.

### 5.8 The feedback note

After the event, the strongest judges write a short *feedback note* to each team they scored. The note is not required; the note distinguishes the strong judges from the average.

Format:

```
"[Team name] — your build was strong on [dimension] because
[specific reason]. The growth area I noticed was [specific
dimension]. If you build again, I'd be curious to see [specific
follow-on]. — [judge name]"
```

Four sentences. ~60 words. Delivered via the event's feedback channel (usually Devpost comments, the cohort Discord, or a shared spreadsheet of feedback).

The feedback note is the post-event extension of the judging seat. The judge who writes feedback notes builds long-term relationships with future-cohort builders; the judge who scores and disappears is forgotten.

# Exercise 3 — The Cue Drill

> **Time:** ~30 minutes.
> **When in the week:** Friday (after Exercise 2).
> **Deliverable:** `EXERCISE-03-CUE-DRILL.md` committed to your `week-09/` working folder.
> **Prerequisite:** Lecture 3 read; the four cues (clipboard, eye-contact, lean-back, side-glance) memorised.

## What this exercise is

You will match four judge body-language cues to their four named recovery moves (Lecture 3 §2.2 through §2.6). For each cue, you will:

- Re-state the cue in your own words.
- State the named recovery move.
- Write a 3-5 sentence vignette describing a scenario in *your team's specific build's pitch* where the cue could appear, and the recovery move you would deploy.

Then write three composite vignettes — short paragraphs (under 100 words each) — for three specific cue + pitch-beat combinations from the matrix below.

The exercise is solo. The vignettes are the rehearsal of recognising the cue in *your* pitch, not in a generic pitch.

## What this exercise is not

This is not a body-language theory exercise. You are not researching human-behaviour studies. The exercise is the *application* of the four named cues to your specific build.

The exercise is also not a memorisation exercise. The four cue names and the four recovery moves are short; the muscle is the *vignette construction*, which forces you to imagine the cue appearing in a beat-specific moment of your pitch.

## What you will need

- Your team's Week 8 pitch script (the script you used for the dress rehearsal at hour 20).
- The four cues and four recovery moves from Lecture 3 §2.
- A 30-minute focus window.

## Part 1 — The cue/recovery matrix

For each cue, complete this block:

```markdown
## Cue [N] — [name from Lecture 3 §2.2-§2.6]

**Cue (one sentence in your own words):**
[Your phrasing of what the judge does physically.]

**Named recovery move:**
[The specific move from Lecture 3 §2.3-§2.6.]

**Where in our pitch this cue is most likely to appear:**
[A specific second-mark in the team's pitch, e.g., "1:15 of the demo
beat, when we are showing the secondary feature."]

**Recovery move applied to our pitch:**
[2-3 sentences on the specific thing we would say or do in response.]
```

Four blocks. About 4-5 minutes per block.

## Part 2 — The composite vignettes

Pick three of the following cue + pitch-beat combinations. Write a short paragraph (60-100 words) for each describing the scenario and the recovery move in real time.

```
Combination A: Clipboard pickup at 0:25 (end of problem beat).
Combination B: Clipboard pickup at 2:10 (early tech beat).
Combination C: Eye-contact loss at 1:00 (middle of demo beat).
Combination D: Eye-contact loss at 2:20 (mid tech beat).
Combination E: Lean-back at 2:30 (late tech beat).
Combination F: Lean-back during Q&A on a follow-up question.
Combination G: Side-glance at 0:35 (just after the demo opens).
Combination H: Side-glance during Q&A on a technical question.
```

Pick three. The variance across the three you pick teaches the *generalisation* — the recovery move is the same family of move, applied to different beats.

```markdown
## Vignette [N] — [Combination letter + cue + beat]

[60-100 words narrating the scenario: what the team is saying, what
the judge does, what cue the team reads, what recovery move the team
deploys, and what changes in the next 5-10 seconds.]
```

Three vignettes. About 5-7 minutes per vignette.

## Acceptance checklist

- [ ] All four cues have a Part-1 block with the cue, the named recovery move, the most-likely pitch moment, and the recovery-move-applied paragraph.
- [ ] Three Part-2 vignettes are written, each 60-100 words.
- [ ] At least two of the three vignettes cover different pitch beats (so the team rehearses recovery across the arc, not just in one beat).
- [ ] At least one of the three vignettes covers a Q&A cue (combinations F or H), not a pitch-beat cue.
- [ ] The committed file is under 250 lines.

## Why this exercise

Reading the room is a probabilistic skill (Lecture 3 §2.7). The team that has *named* the cues and *rehearsed* the recovery moves reads the room better than the team that has not. The exercise converts the lecture content into beat-specific muscle memory.

By Saturday's mock pitch in the mini-project, the team should be able to:
- Recognise any of the four cues in real time.
- Trigger the named recovery move within 1-2 seconds of recognition.
- Resume the pitch without losing the next beat's content.

The exercise is the bench; the mini-project is the run.

## A note on what vignettes are "right"

The vignettes are graded on three properties:

- **Beat-specificity.** The vignette names a specific second-mark and a specific beat, not a generic "during the demo."
- **Recovery-move concreteness.** The vignette names the *specific thing the team says or does* in the recovery, not a vague "the team adjusts."
- **Honesty.** The vignette is plausible for the team's actual pitch script, not a generic textbook example.

The solutions in [SOLUTIONS.md](./SOLUTIONS.md) show three sample vignettes for a fake build. Your build is different; your vignettes will differ. The metric is whether *your* vignettes have the three properties above for *your* pitch.

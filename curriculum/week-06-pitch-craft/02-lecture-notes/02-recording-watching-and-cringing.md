# Lecture 2 — Recording, Watching, and Cringing

> **Duration:** ~1 hour of reading.
> **Outcome:** You can record a three-minute demo against the live URL in one take using free tools. You can watch the first take honestly — at 1.0x and at 1.5x, with audio and without — and write the three notes that drive the second take. You can re-record once, compare the two takes against the demo-timing strip, and commit the final recording with a public link in the repo README. You can name the four watching-yourself failure modes and the test that catches each one.

If you only remember one sentence from this lecture, remember this:

> **The script is what you write at your desk; the recording is what you learn by watching yourself. The cringe of the first take is the data; the three notes you write while watching are the second take's improvements. Most pitches need three to four takes to land. The two-take minimum is the floor, not the ceiling.**

Lecture 1 taught you the script — five beats, four boundaries, one page. Lecture 2 teaches you the *recording* — the artifact that the script becomes when you point a camera at the live URL and read your lines. The script reads clean on the page. The recording reveals which lines were lazy, which beats overran, and which transitions you faked. The first take is the data; the second is the artifact.

---

## 1. Why record at all this week

The first reaction beginners have to "record yourself this week" is: *I'll record at the event.* I know my build. I have the script. The real recording is at hour 33 of the hackathon.

That reaction is wrong on every count. Here is the steel-man:

> **C4 voice:** the first take of any demo is bad. Always. The first take is the data — it is where you discover the script you wrote does not match the script you read aloud. Discovering that at hour 33 of an event costs you the demo. Discovering it on Thursday of Week 6 costs you 40 minutes of re-recording. The compound is brutal in one direction and trivial in the other.

### 1.1 What the recording rehearsal actually does

Three short sentences, one per outcome:

- **The recording surfaces the over-run.** A 415-word script reads as 3:00 in your head and as 3:45 on the recording. The 45 extra seconds are the gap between read-silently-on-the-page and read-aloud-with-clicking. Only the recording surfaces them.
- **The recording surfaces the silent clicks.** The solution beat's narration sounds clean in your head; on the recording, you fall silent at click 2 for 9 seconds while the page loads. Nine seconds of silence is a hole in the demo. Only the recording surfaces it.
- **The recording surfaces the missing ask.** You read the ask in your head and the closing "thanks for watching" feels natural. On the recording, it lands flat. Only the recording surfaces it.

If the recording rehearsal does not surface any of these, the rehearsal failed — you recorded but did not *watch*. Watching is half the rehearsal.

### 1.2 The two-take minimum

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  THE TWO-TAKE MINIMUM — Week 6 Default                     │
│                                                            │
│  Take 1 (Thursday)  Record on the live URL. One take, no   │
│                     restart. Watch it once at 1.0x. Write   │
│                     three notes.                            │
│                                                            │
│  Take 2 (Friday)    Apply the three notes. Re-record. Watch │
│                     once at 1.0x. Compare to take 1.        │
│                                                            │
│  (Take 3 — Saturday, optional but typical)                  │
│  (Take 4 — Sunday, for the cohort review)                   │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The two-take minimum is the floor. Most pitches need three to four takes to land. The Saturday third take is where most C4 alumni report the recording felt *delivered* rather than *recited*. The fourth take, optional, is for cohort feedback.

The takes are *sequential*, not parallel. You do not record three back-to-back; you record, watch, write notes, then record again. The watch-and-note is the muscle. Without it, the second take is a re-recitation of the first.

## 2. The recording setup

Before you press record, you need four things in place. None of them require money; all of them require five minutes of setup.

### 2.1 The four pre-recording checks

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  THE FOUR PRE-RECORDING CHECKS                             │
│                                                            │
│  CHECK 1 — The live URL is reachable.                      │
│    Open the deploy URL in a fresh browser tab. Confirm it  │
│    loads in under 3 seconds. Confirm the MUST verb works.  │
│                                                            │
│  CHECK 2 — The script is one page, printed or on screen.   │
│    Hook + problem + solution-outline + ask. ~415 words.    │
│    On a second monitor or printed sheet — not in the same  │
│    window you are recording.                               │
│                                                            │
│  CHECK 3 — The recording tool is ready and tested.         │
│    Loom / OBS / QuickTime / Game Bar — pick one. Record a  │
│    10-second test clip. Confirm audio is clear, video is   │
│    crisp, the live URL is visible.                         │
│                                                            │
│  CHECK 4 — The room is quiet.                              │
│    Door closed, fan low, phone on silent, roommate notified│
│    Twenty minutes of quiet for one take + one watch.       │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

If any of the four checks fail, fix it before pressing record. The fix is mechanical: re-deploy the URL, print the script, restart the recording tool, ask the roommate. Five minutes total; the recording is durable.

### 2.2 The "live URL is the backdrop" rule (repeat)

> **C4 voice:** the solution beat is filmed against the live URL, never against slides. The hook and problem beat may be filmed against a slide (a single title card) or against the pitcher's face; the solution beat must show the URL moving.

Three setup options, in order of recommendation:

- **Screen recording with face camera in corner.** Loom does this by default. The screen is the solution beat backdrop; the face camera is the pitcher's face during the hook and ask. This is the C4 default.
- **Screen recording only.** OBS or QuickTime, no face overlay. The screen is the entire backdrop. Acceptable; the pitch loses 5% of its warmth without a face.
- **Phone camera pointed at the laptop.** A crude setup; works when no screen recorder is available. The audio is the limiter; record audio separately if the laptop fan is loud.

The setup is the tool; the tool is not the skill. Pick one in 5 minutes; do not spend 30 minutes optimizing the setup.

### 2.3 The "one take, no restart" rule

The first recording is one take. You do not stop mid-take. You do not restart at the apology. You do not re-record the hook because the first sentence stumbled. You let the whole take run. The mistakes are the data.

> **C4 voice:** restarts compress the take into a "best-of" recording that is not a real demo. At the event, you do not get to restart. The Thursday take simulates the event. Let it run. The fluffs are the lesson.

The exceptions: a technical failure (the live URL goes down mid-take, the recording tool crashes, the audio dropped). Then restart. Not for human fluffs.

## 3. The first take

Press record. Open the demo-timing strip on screen (a second monitor, a printout, a tab you can glance at). Start the timer. Begin with the hook.

### 3.1 The five-minute window

The full take is 3 minutes. Allow yourself 2 minutes of buffer: 30 seconds before the take (settle, breathe, start the timer) and 90 seconds after (let the screen recording continue past 3:00, then stop). A 5-minute window is the recording slot.

Block the 20 minutes around the take: 5 for setup, 5 for the take itself, 10 for the first watch. Do not try to record at a 5-minute window between meetings.

### 3.2 The hook — first 30 seconds of the recording

The hook is hardest to deliver on take 1 because there is no prior beat to ride into it. You open cold. Three calibrations:

- **Look at the camera, not the screen.** The hook is talking to a person, not narrating a build. The screen comes alive at 1:15.
- **Speak slower than feels natural.** First-take hooks are 30% faster than they feel; the cap is 30 seconds for three lines, not 22 seconds.
- **Hit the verb in line 3.** The verb is the handoff to the problem beat. If line 3 mumbles past the verb, the beat boundary blurs.

### 3.3 The problem beat — seconds 30 to 75

The problem beat is the *narrowing*. The hook named the user in scenes; the problem beat names the specific user, the specific time, the specific cost.

Two calibrations:

- **Cite a number, even an imagined one.** "Four unanswered questions a week" is concrete; "lots of questions" is vague. Cite the number even if you have not measured it; the discipline is to name a quantity.
- **End on the handoff sentence.** The last 5 seconds of the problem beat is the handoff — "this is the moment X is built for." That sentence is the cue for the live URL to come on screen.

### 3.4 The solution beat — seconds 75 to 165

The solution beat is three clicks on the live URL. The narration is one or two sentences per click. The click is the verb. The visible result is the demo.

Three calibrations:

- **Practice the click cadence beforehand.** Open the live URL. Click click click. Time it. If the three clicks take 12 seconds with no narration, you have 78 seconds for narration distributed across the beat — about 26 seconds per click of narration. Plan for that.
- **Pause for the result.** When the third click produces the result, hold silent for 2 seconds. Let the audience see it. Then narrate the demo-clip line.
- **Cut to a code snippet only if it adds 5 seconds and a name.** "Here is where we call the [Sponsor] API, in one file, in 12 lines." Then back to the URL. Most demos do not need this cut.

### 3.5 The ask — seconds 165 to 180

The ask is 15 seconds. Two sentences. Read it. Then stop. Then close the recording.

The most-common first-take failure on the ask is *speeding through it*. The pitcher has been talking for 165 seconds and wants to be done; the ask compresses to 6 seconds and the demo loses the last beat. Slow down. The last 15 seconds is the highest-leverage 15 seconds of the recording.

## 4. Watching the first take

You recorded. Now you watch. This is the part most learners skip, and it is the part where the actual learning happens.

### 4.1 The four watches

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  THE FOUR WATCHES OF A FIRST TAKE                          │
│                                                            │
│  WATCH 1 — 1.0x, audio on, video on (3 minutes)            │
│    The full demo at normal speed. Take notes on pacing.    │
│                                                            │
│  WATCH 2 — 1.5x, audio on, video on (2 minutes)            │
│    Speed-watch. Take notes on structural drift.            │
│                                                            │
│  WATCH 3 — 1.0x, audio off, video on (3 minutes)           │
│    Visual audit. Is the live URL visible on the solution   │
│    beat? Are the clicks crisp?                             │
│                                                            │
│  WATCH 4 — 1.0x, audio on, video off (3 minutes)           │
│    Audio audit. Does the hook land? Does the ask name a    │
│    number and a channel?                                   │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

Four watches total. About 12 minutes. The four watches catch four different layers of issues; one combined watch catches one layer (pacing) and misses three. Budget the 12 minutes.

### 4.2 The "cringe is the data" rule

You will cringe. The first take is bad. That is what first takes are. The discomfort is the highest-signal feedback you will get this week; sit through it.

> **C4 voice:** the pitcher who refuses to watch their first take is the pitcher who shows up at the event with the same mistakes. The cringe is the cost of admission. Pay it; the second take is cheaper for it.

Three things the cringe catches that you will not catch otherwise:

- **Filler words.** "Um," "like," "so basically," "you know." Count them on watch 1. Three or more in the hook is a re-write.
- **Trailing sentences.** Sentences that fade at the end ("...so yeah," "...kind of"). Trailing sentences read as unsure. Note them on watch 4 (audio only); they are louder without the video.
- **Stagnant screens.** Moments where the live URL is loading and the pitcher is silent. Note them on watch 3 (video only); 5+ seconds of stagnant screen is a hole.

### 4.3 The three-note rule

After the four watches, write *three* notes. Not five. Not ten. Three.

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  THE THREE-NOTE RULE                                       │
│                                                            │
│  Note 1: the worst-performing beat (hook, problem,         │
│           solution, or ask) and the one-sentence fix.      │
│  Note 2: the most common filler word or trailing pattern   │
│           (a habit; the fix is awareness, not script).     │
│  Note 3: one structural or timing fix (the beat that       │
│           overran or underran by more than 15 seconds).    │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The three notes are the *budget* for the second take. Three changes is the right number — enough to improve, few enough to remember while recording. A list of 10 changes produces a stiff second take where the pitcher is performing the corrections, not delivering the demo.

### 4.4 The four watching-yourself failure modes

Four predictable failure modes for watching yourself. Recognize them in your own first-watch.

#### 4.4.1 The "I sound terrible" spiral

> **Symptom:** the learner watches 10 seconds, hates the sound of their voice, closes the recording, gives up.

The voice on the recording is the voice everyone hears. It is not the voice you hear in your head. Both are real; the recording is the one the audience gets. Get past the first 30 seconds of the watch; the rest is structural feedback, not voice feedback.

The fix: watch the full take before judging it. The watch is for the structure; the voice is the byproduct.

#### 4.4.2 The fast-forward

> **Symptom:** the learner watches at 2x to "get through it faster."

Two-times speed compresses pacing issues into a blur. You will not catch the 9-second silence in the solution beat at 2x; you will catch it at 1.0x. The 1.5x watch is the second pass, not the first.

The fix: watch 1 is 1.0x. Always. The 1.5x watch comes second.

#### 4.4.3 The polish-over-structure note

> **Symptom:** all three notes are about wording ("change 'gets her' to 'sends her'") instead of structure.

Wording changes are easy; they are the lowest-value fixes. The high-value fixes are structural: the hook does not name the user, the solution beat is a feature tour, the ask is missing. Notes that focus on wording miss the higher-leverage fixes.

The fix: write at least two of the three notes about *structure* (the demo-timing strip), not wording. Wording can be polished on take 3.

#### 4.4.4 The "I'll fix it at the event" delay

> **Symptom:** the learner watches the take, sees the issues, and says "I'll fix this at the event."

You will not. The event is louder, faster, and more anxious than your bedroom on Thursday. The fixes have to happen *now*, on Friday, while the cost of re-recording is 20 minutes. At the event, the cost of re-recording is the demo session.

The fix: schedule take 2. On the calendar. On Friday. Twenty minutes blocked. The fix is the calendar, not willpower.

## 5. The second take

Friday. You recorded once. You watched four times. You wrote three notes. Now you re-record.

### 5.1 The three-note re-read

Before pressing record, re-read the three notes out loud. The verbal re-read commits them to working memory in a way silent reading does not. The take that follows will carry the corrections without your needing to consciously hit them mid-pitch.

### 5.2 The re-record itself

Same setup as take 1. Same four pre-recording checks. Same one-take rule. The only differences:

- **You know the script better.** The hook flows. The handoff sentence lands. The ask does not rush.
- **You catch the filler words mid-take.** Awareness from watching catches at least half of the "um" and "like" by take 2.
- **The pacing is closer to 3:00.** First takes run 3:30–3:50; second takes usually land at 2:55–3:10.

### 5.3 The compare

After take 2, watch both takes back to back. Take 1 first, then take 2. The contrast is the lesson.

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  THE TAKE-1 vs TAKE-2 COMPARISON                           │
│                                                            │
│  Hook length:     T1 ___ sec / T2 ___ sec   (target 30)    │
│  Problem length:  T1 ___ sec / T2 ___ sec   (target 45)    │
│  Solution length: T1 ___ sec / T2 ___ sec   (target 90)    │
│  Ask length:      T1 ___ sec / T2 ___ sec   (target 15)    │
│  Total length:    T1 ___ sec / T2 ___ sec   (target 180)   │
│                                                            │
│  Worst beat:      T1 ________ / T2 ________                │
│  Filler count:    T1 __       / T2 __                      │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The numbers are the audit. If T2 is closer to the target on every line, take 2 is the artifact. If T2 regressed on a line, that line is the focus of take 3.

### 5.4 When to record take 3

You record take 3 if:

- Take 2 still overruns by more than 15 seconds. The cap is 180 seconds; 195+ is over.
- Take 2 still has a missing or vague ask. The last 15 seconds is the highest-leverage beat to fix.
- Take 2's solution beat is still a feature tour. The cut-to-three has not landed yet.
- The cohort review on Sunday flags a structural issue that take 2 does not fix.

Most learners record take 3 on Saturday. The 24-hour gap between take 2 and take 3 is the value — your tongue forgets the script enough to *deliver* it rather than *recite* it. The gap is the secret.

## 6. The recording host — where the artifact lives

The recording is an artifact. Like the contract and the worksheet, it has a canonical location. The choice of host matters less than the durability of the link.

### 6.1 The host options

Re-read the resources page for the full list. Summary:

- **YouTube unlisted.** C4 default for the final submission. Durable URL, no sign-up wall, embeds in READMEs.
- **Loom public.** Faster to upload; durable enough for a 10-week course. Free tier shows small Loom branding.
- **Vimeo free.** Cleaner player than YouTube; lower upload caps.
- **A direct `.mp4` in your repo.** GitHub's 100 MB file limit is the constraint. A 3-minute 720p screen recording is usually 30–80 MB.

C4's mini-project default is **YouTube unlisted**. The link is the artifact; the repo README has it on line 1 of the "Demo" section.

### 6.2 The "no sign-up wall" rule

> **C4 voice:** the judge has 90 seconds. The sign-up wall is the failure. If a viewer cannot click play within 5 seconds of arriving at the URL, the host failed. Pick a host that respects the viewer's time.

This rule excludes some otherwise-good hosts:

- **Wistia free.** Often inserts a sign-up gate after 10 seconds. Skip.
- **Personal Google Drive videos.** Require a Google sign-in for some viewers depending on sharing settings. Risky.
- **A Notion-embedded video.** Works, but the loading of the Notion page is the first sign-up wall.

The three C4-approved defaults (YouTube unlisted, Loom public, Vimeo free) all skip the sign-up wall by design. Pick one.

### 6.3 The repo README link

The final recording link goes into the repo README in a section labeled `## Demo`. One line. The URL. Nothing else on the first line of the section.

```markdown
## Demo

[Watch the 3-minute demo](https://youtube.com/watch?v=...)
```

The link is what the judge clicks. The link is what the cohort shares. The link is what the portfolio reader finds. If the link is buried six lines down, it is not found.

## 7. The demo-script + recording feeds Week 7

Week 7 is the first 6-hour mock hackathon dry-run. The recording skill you built in Week 6 is used at hour 6 of Week 7: the team pitches its 6-hour mock build using the same demo-timing strip, recorded the same way.

### 7.1 What transfers cleanly

- **The strip itself.** 30s hook / 45s problem / 90s solution / 15s ask. Same five beats, same four boundaries, same one page of script. The build is different; the strip is the same.
- **The two-take minimum.** At Week 7's hour 6, the team records take 1, watches it together, writes three notes, re-records. The cycle is the same as solo this week.
- **The first-sentence audit and the last-sentence audit.** Same audits, run on the team's hook and ask. The audits do not require teammates; one teammate runs each in 30 seconds.
- **The four watches.** Same four watches, run on the team's take 1.

### 7.2 What needs new muscle (in Week 7)

- **Speaking on behalf of a team.** "We built X" replaces "I built X." The pronoun is the only difference; the rest of the script is identical.
- **Handing off mid-beat.** A team of 4 may have one pitcher (the C4 default; cleaner pacing) or two (one for hook/problem, one for solution/ask). The handoff at 1:15 is the new boundary.
- **Recording with a team in the room.** The single biggest change. The pitcher records while the team watches and silently scores; the cringe is now public. Week 7's dry-run is where this is rehearsed.

This is why Week 6 sits where it does. The recording skill is solo this week; the team adapts it next week. The structure (the strip) carries across; the delivery (solo vs team) is the muscle that grows.

## 8. Reading other people's recordings

A late skill: watching a *different person's* three-minute demo is one of the most under-used pitch study habits. Most public hackathon submissions have a recorded demo. Watching three of those across the week sharpens your eye for the strip in a way reading lectures does not.

### 8.1 What to look for

- **The hook's first sentence.** Does it name a user and a pain in the first 8 seconds? Most pitches do not. The ones that do are the ones that win the demo session.
- **The 1:15 boundary.** Does the live URL appear on screen at 1:15? Or does the pitcher spend 90 seconds on the problem and never get to the demo? The boundary is the test.
- **The ask.** Does the closing 15 seconds contain a number and a channel? Or does it close with "thanks for watching"? The ask is the rarest beat done well.

### 8.2 What to ignore

- **The production quality.** A recording with great audio and a polished overlay is not necessarily a better demo than a phone-camera shaky recording. The strip is the bench, not the production.
- **The tech-stack mentions.** "We used React and FastAPI and a vector DB" is almost always irrelevant to the demo. The strip cares about user, pain, verb, ask; the stack is the README.
- **The team size cited.** "We were a team of 8" vs "team of 2" does not change the strip. The 3-minute clock is the same.

Homework Problem 3 this week asks you to watch three real hackathon demos and produce a 150-word audit. The skill builds fast with reps.

## 9. The recording is the artifact

Every habit in this lecture — the four pre-recording checks, the one-take rule, the four watches, the three-note rule, the two-take minimum — needs to be *captured* in the artifact you commit at the end of the week. That artifact is the **3-minute recorded demo of your Week-3 solo prototype**, hosted publicly, linked in the repo README.

The recording is not optional. A learner without a recorded demo is a learner who arrives at the next event having *thought about* their pitch; the ones who win the demo session have *recorded and watched* themselves at least twice. The cost of recording this week is 90 minutes; the cost of not recording is the demo session at the real event.

### 9.1 What goes in the artifact

The artifact is the recording itself, plus three lines in the repo README:

1. The recording URL (YouTube unlisted, Loom public, or Vimeo).
2. A one-line caption: "3-minute demo of the Week-3 solo prototype, recorded for C4 Week 6."
3. The take count: "Take N of N." (e.g., "Take 2 of 2" or "Take 3 of 4"). This is the C4 voice's transparency rule — the recording is honest about being a rehearsal, not a performance.

The mini-project this week is the recording, hosted, and the three-line README block. Saturday's session is the recording; Sunday's polish is the README and the cohort post.

## 10. Recap

- The recording is what you learn by watching yourself. The script reads clean on the page; the recording reveals which lines were lazy and which beats overran.
- The two-take minimum is the floor. Most pitches need 3 to 4 takes. The 24-hour gap between takes is where the delivery sharpens.
- The four pre-recording checks are mechanical: live URL reachable, script printed, recording tool tested, room quiet. Five minutes of setup; the recording is durable.
- The one-take rule is the discipline. You let the take run; the fluffs are the data. Restarts compress the take into a "best-of" that is not a real demo.
- The four watches catch four different layers: pacing (1.0x audio+video), structural drift (1.5x audio+video), visual gaps (audio off), and verbal gaps (video off). Twelve minutes total per take.
- The three-note rule is the budget. Three notes per watch; not five, not ten. Three changes is the right size for take 2 to land without becoming stiff.
- Four watching-yourself failure modes: the "I sound terrible" spiral, the fast-forward, the polish-over-structure note, the "fix it at the event" delay. Tests for all four; learn them.
- The host is YouTube unlisted by default. No sign-up walls. The recording link goes in the repo README on line 1 of the `## Demo` section.
- The demo-script + recording feeds Week 7's team-mode dry-run: same strip, same audits, same four watches, same two-take minimum. Solo this week; team next.

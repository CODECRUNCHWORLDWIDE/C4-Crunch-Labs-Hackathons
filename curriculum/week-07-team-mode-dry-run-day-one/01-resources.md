# Week 7 — Resources

Every resource on this page is **free** and **publicly accessible**. No paywalled books, no signups beyond a free tier. If a link breaks, please open an issue.

## Required reading and watching (work it into your week)

- **Major League Hacking (MLH) — "Hackathon Organizer Guide" (public sections).** The MLH organizer guide includes a multi-chapter public set on participant expectations, code of conduct, day-of-event run sheets, and the standard 24-/36-/48-hour event cadence. The *Run Sheet* chapter is the closest free reference to a real event's hour-by-hour kickoff schedule. Read the kickoff and judging chapters once. Search: `MLH hackathon organizer guide` → <https://organize.mlh.io/>
- **MLH — Code of Conduct.** A short, plain-language document. Required reading because the dry-run's comms norms inherit from it. <https://mlh.io/code-of-conduct>
- **Atlassian — "Agile coach" free articles, specifically the daily stand-up and retrospective guides.** Two short articles, ~10 minutes each. The stand-up article is the template the dry-run's three stand-ups inherit from. The retrospective article frames the hour-24 close-out. <https://www.atlassian.com/agile/scrum/standups> and <https://www.atlassian.com/agile/scrum/retrospectives>
- **Devpost — "Hackathon participant guide."** Devpost's documentation on what counts as a started-during-the-event project, submission rules, and the judging window. The dry-run rehearses these constraints. <https://help.devpost.com/hc/en-us/categories/360001712491>
- **GitHub — "About teams" and "Best practices for repositories."** The dry-run team works in one repo with multiple committers; the GitHub docs on teams and protected branches are the minimum read before hour 0. <https://docs.github.com/en/organizations/organizing-members-into-teams/about-teams>

If you only have time for one, read MLH's Run Sheet chapter and the Atlassian stand-up article back-to-back. Twenty minutes of reading; the kickoff is half-written by the time you finish.

## On hackathon team dynamics (free, deeper)

| Piece | Why | Link |
|------|-----|------|
| **MLH — "How to recruit a hackathon team"** | A blog post on how MLH-affiliated events expect teams to form, the four-person cap rationale, and the rules for late team-up. | Search: `MLH how to recruit hackathon team` |
| **GitHub Education Blog — student-team retrospectives** | First-person posts from past Major League Hacking and university-event participants. The good ones name what broke at hour 4 and what the team did differently at hour 16. | <https://github.blog/category/community/education/> |
| **Atlassian — "Forming, Storming, Norming, Performing"** | Bruce Tuckman's classic four-stage team model, re-applied to short-term projects. | **MIT OCW — 6.S974 / similar open team-project materials** | MIT OpenCourseWare's free team-project syllabi name the team-charter, role-assignment, and conflict-handling artifacts the dry-run uses. The OCW materials are public-domain-reusable; copy what is useful. | <https://ocw.mit.edu/> |

The Atlassian team-formation page is the single most-cited public artifact for short-team dynamics. It is short, free, and most learners have not read it through. Read it once before Tuesday's exercise.

## On kickoff meetings and agendas

- **MLH Organizer Guide — "Opening Ceremony" template.** The opening ceremony agenda for an MLH event is the structural template for the dry-run's kickoff. The MLH version is 30 minutes (more attendees); the dry-run's is 60 minutes (smaller team, more decisions). Same beats. <https://organize.mlh.io/>
- **Atlassian Team Playbook — "Roles and Responsibilities."** A free template for surfacing role expectations in a 30-minute meeting. The dry-run uses this template (compressed to 10 minutes) for the role-assignment segment of the kickoff. <https://www.atlassian.com/team-playbook/plays/roles-and-responsibilities>
- **Google re:Work — "Guide: Run effective meetings."** Google's open team-effectiveness guide. The "agenda first" rule and the "decisions vs discussion" framing apply directly to the kickoff. <https://rework.withgoogle.com/guides/managers-coach-managers-to-coach/steps/run-effective-meetings/>

The kickoff is a meeting; the meeting has an agenda; the agenda is timed. Three resources, one rule.

## On the three roles — Builder Lead, Demo Lead, Comms Lead

C4's three-role model is a compression of the larger hackathon-team taxonomy. For deeper background on each role, see:

| Role | Background reading | Link |
|------|--------------------|------|
| **Builder Lead** | GitHub's "Best practices for a healthy repository" — branching, PR review, merge cadence. | <https://docs.github.com/en/get-started/quickstart/github-flow> |
| **Demo Lead** | Devpost's "Tips for recording a demo video" (also referenced in W6). | <https://info.devpost.com/blog/hackathon-demo-videos> |
| **Comms Lead** | Slack's "Channel naming and etiquette" guide, plus the Atlassian "async-first communication" article. | <https://slack.com/help/articles/217626408-Create-a-channel> and <https://www.atlassian.com/blog/teamwork/asynchronous-communication> |

The roles are not job titles; they are *deciders*. The Builder Lead decides what merges to main and when. The Demo Lead decides what goes in the three-minute recording. The Comms Lead decides what gets posted in the channel and what stays in DMs. Decider-not-doer is the key distinction; read the linked articles with that lens.

## On scoping and parallelization

- **C4 Week 5 — `SCOPING-WORKSHEET.md`.** The team re-runs the Week-5 worksheet at hour 2 of the dry-run. Re-read the Week-5 README's MoSCoW section once.
- **Atlassian — "How to write user stories."** The free guide is the closest public match to the worksheet's MUST-row format. The team-mode adaptation in Lecture 2 §3 references this guide. <https://www.atlassian.com/agile/project-management/user-stories>
- **GitHub Projects — free for student dev pack users.** A lightweight kanban board for tracking the MUST list during the dry-run. Three columns: TODO / DOING / DONE. <https://docs.github.com/en/issues/planning-and-tracking-with-projects/learning-about-projects/about-projects>

The scope worksheet is the artifact. The kanban board is the live state. The two together are the team's shared state model.

## On communication norms

- **Slack — "Channel etiquette" and "DM vs channel" docs.** Short, plain-language guidance. The DM-vs-channel rule the dry-run enforces — *decisions in channel, banter in DMs* — is from the Slack docs adapted to the C4 voice. <https://slack.com/help/articles/360017938993-What-is-Slack->
- **Discord — "Setting up a small team server."** If your team uses Discord instead of Slack, the Discord docs cover the same ground. The norms transfer directly; the tool does not matter. <https://support.discord.com/hc/en-us/categories/200404398>
- **Atlassian — "Asynchronous communication."** A short read on why async is the default and synchronous is the exception. The dry-run treats the three stand-ups as synchronous exceptions inside an otherwise async window. <https://www.atlassian.com/blog/teamwork/asynchronous-communication>

The norm rule: one channel, one doc, one stand-up time, one DM rule. Four norms; one paragraph each in your `TEAM-CONTRACT.md`.

## On conflict resolution at speed

- **Crucial Conversations — "What is at stake" framework (public summary articles, no purchase required).** The book is paid; the public summaries on the free-article side of the publisher's site cover the relevant frame: high-stakes, opposing-opinion, strong-emotion conversations. The four day-1 conflicts in Lecture 3 fit this frame exactly. Search: `crucial conversations summary opposing opinion`
- **MIT Sloan — "Conflict management in teams" (open lecture slides on OCW).** A free slide deck on the four primary conflict modes (task, process, relationship, status) and the timing of each. The dry-run's four named conflicts map to task and process modes — the easier two to resolve under time pressure. <https://ocw.mit.edu/>
- **Atlassian Team Playbook — "Working agreements" play.** Free template for a 30-minute working-agreements session. The dry-run compresses this to 10 minutes and treats the output as part of the `TEAM-CONTRACT.md`. <https://www.atlassian.com/team-playbook/plays/working-agreements>

The conflicts in Lecture 3 are *not* relationship conflicts. They are task and process conflicts wearing the costume of relationship conflicts. The reading distinguishes the two.

## On stand-ups during a hackathon

- **Atlassian — "Daily stand-up" guide.** The 15-minute stand-up format is the source template. The dry-run shrinks it to 10 minutes (smaller team) and runs it three times in 24 hours (instead of daily). <https://www.atlassian.com/agile/scrum/standups>
- **Spotify Engineering — "Squad standups" blog (archived posts).** The Spotify engineering team's public posts on what worked and what did not. The relevant reading is the part about *async stand-ups* — the dry-run uses async stand-ups for the off-hour periods. <https://engineering.atspotify.com/>

The stand-up is three questions. What did you ship? What is blocking? What is the next 4 hours? Ten minutes total. One note-taker. The note goes in the log.

## On the hour-24 close-out

- **Atlassian — "Sprint retrospective" guide.** A free template for a 30-minute retrospective. The dry-run's hour-24 close-out is a 30-minute retrospective in disguise — same four quadrants (start, stop, continue, more of) compressed into the three named outputs (what shipped, what is on day-2, team-health word). <https://www.atlassian.com/agile/scrum/retrospectives>
- **Google re:Work — "Retrospective: Discuss successes and challenges."** Google's open retrospective template. Useful as a cross-check on the Atlassian format. <https://rework.withgoogle.com/guides/teams-foster-psychological-safety/steps/>

The close-out is short. The log entry is shorter. Both fit on one page.

## Tools you will use this week (no paid installs)

- **A chat tool.** Slack free / Discord / Microsoft Teams free / Element / a shared text thread. Pick one; the entire team uses the same one. Do not span two tools.
- **A shared doc tool.** Google Docs (free with any Google account) / HackMD / GitHub wiki / a shared file in the repo. Pick one.
- **A repo with multi-committer access.** GitHub free public repo / GitLab free / Codeberg / Sourcehut. The dry-run team needs everyone to be able to push (or open PRs that merge fast). GitHub is the default.
- **A video call tool.** Google Meet free (any Google account, 60-minute cap for 3+ participants) / Discord voice / Zoom free / Jitsi. The kickoff and the three stand-ups need video or at least voice.
- **A timer.** Phone timer, kitchen timer, browser timer. The 60-minute kickoff cap and the 10-minute stand-up cap are mechanical disciplines. Run the timer in-shot on the call.
- **A shared kanban or task list.** GitHub Projects (free for student dev pack) / GitHub Issues / a Markdown checklist in the repo. Three columns is enough.

The GitHub Student Developer Pack — <https://education.github.com/pack> — is free with a verified student email and includes a free Pro tier on GitHub, Notion, and several other tools. None of them are required for the dry-run; the free tiers of the base tools suffice.

## On the dry-run logistics

- **Time-zone coordinator.** If the team spans time zones, use <https://www.timeanddate.com/worldclock/converter.html> to plan the kickoff and three stand-ups. Write the timestamps in the log in UTC plus the team's primary time zone.
- **Calendar invite.** Google Calendar (free) or any calendar app. The kickoff and the three stand-ups go on the calendar before the dry-run starts.
- **A pinned message in the team channel.** Pin the agenda for the kickoff. Pin the stand-up template. Pin the comms norms paragraph from `TEAM-CONTRACT.md`. Three pinned messages.

Logistics is not glamour. Logistics is the half of a hackathon kickoff that beginners skip and then spend hour 4 paying for. Front-load it.

## Examples of strong dry-runs (study samples)

The single best study material is *other teams' day-1 logs*. Three sources, in order of usefulness:

| Source | What you find | How to search |
|--------|---------------|---------------|
| **The C4 cohort archive — past dry-run logs** | Other cohort teams' committed `DAY-1-LOG.md` files. Read three. The honest ones name a conflict; the dishonest ones do not. | Check the C4 archive folder in the repo |
| **Public GitHub repos with `hackathon` topic and committed daily logs** | A long tail of teams that committed dev logs during real events. Quality varies; the winners usually have logs. | <https://github.com/topics/hackathon> |
| **Devpost — past winning project repos linked from submissions** | Some winning teams link their working repo from the Devpost submission. The repos with commits during the event window show real-time team-mode coordination. | <https://devpost.com/hackathons?status=ended> |

The reading skill: read three logs this week, write one sentence per log on what their hour-2 scope cut was. Three sentences total. Homework Problem 3 is exactly this exercise.

## On code of conduct and inclusion

- **MLH Code of Conduct.** Required reading. Short, plain-language. The dry-run inherits all of the MLH CoC's behavioral rules. <https://mlh.io/code-of-conduct>
- **Contributor Covenant.** The default open-source code of conduct. The dry-run's repo should have a `CODE_OF_CONDUCT.md` linking to this. <https://www.contributor-covenant.org/>
- **CAHSI — public student-org guidance on inclusion in CS team projects.** CAHSI (Computing Alliance of Hispanic-Serving Institutions) publishes free guidance for inclusion-aware team practices. Useful for the team's hour-0 introductions segment of the kickoff. <https://cahsi.utep.edu/>

The CoC is not decoration. The dry-run is a rehearsal of real-event behavior; if the team's behavior on the dry-run is not CoC-compliant, the team's behavior at the real event will not be either.

## Glossary cheat sheet

| Term | Plain English |
|------|---------------|
| **Dry-run** | A simulated 24-hour hackathon kickoff with a real team, using a written prompt and a real repo. Same artifacts as a real event; lower stakes. |
| **Kickoff meeting** | The 60-minute opening meeting at hour 0 of the dry-run. Six 10-minute segments. The agenda is pinned in the team channel before hour 0. |
| **Builder Lead** | The decider of what merges to main and when. One per team. Rotates at hour 12 on 4-person teams. |
| **Demo Lead** | The decider of what goes in the three-minute team demo recording. One per team. The role's deliverable is the demo script and the recording handoff. |
| **Comms Lead** | The decider of what gets posted in the team channel and what stays in DMs. One per team. The role's deliverable is the channel-pinned messages and the `DAY-1-LOG.md` updates. |
| **Three-track parallelization** | Build track / design+demo track / comms+log track. Three named tracks; one named owner per track; no track blocks another for more than 30 minutes between hour 4 and hour 22. |
| **The 30-minute block rule** | No track waits on another for more than 30 minutes. If a block exceeds 30 minutes, the blocked track switches to its next-priority MUST item or escalates to the Builder Lead. |
| **Stand-up** | A 10-minute synchronous (voice or video) team check at hours 4, 12, and 22. Three questions: what shipped, what is blocking, what is the next 4 hours. One note-taker; the note goes in the log. |
| **Stand-up note** | A timestamped Markdown block in `DAY-1-LOG.md`. One per team member per stand-up. Three bullets each. |
| **One channel, one doc, one stand-up, one DM rule** | The four comms norms set during the kickoff. One chat channel for the team. One shared doc. One stand-up cadence. One rule for when to DM vs post in channel. |
| **The four day-1 conflicts** | Scope-creep, silent disagreement, role-overlap, "I'll just rewrite it" merge fight. Lecture 3 §4 names each and gives a one-sentence intervention. |
| **One-sentence intervention** | A pre-written, low-emotion sentence used to surface a conflict without escalating it. Lecture 3 §4 provides four. |
| **Hour-24 close-out** | The 30-minute meeting at the end of the dry-run. Three named outputs: what shipped vs MUST, the day-2 plan, the team-health one-word check. |
| **`DAY-1-LOG.md`** | The committed Markdown file that records the dry-run. Timestamped entries for the kickoff, the scope pass, each stand-up, each named conflict moment, and the close-out. Co-authored or named-contributor by at least one teammate. |
| **MLH Run Sheet** | The Major League Hacking Organizer Guide's hour-by-hour event schedule. The dry-run's hour-by-hour schedule (Lecture 2 §5) is structurally adapted from this document. |
| **Code of Conduct** | The MLH CoC (or Contributor Covenant) the dry-run's behavior inherits from. The dry-run is a behavior rehearsal as much as a build rehearsal. |
| **Team-health word** | A one-word check at the close-out: each teammate says one word describing how they feel. Not "fine." Words that pass: "tired-but-good," "frustrated," "energized," "behind." Three words; one log line. |

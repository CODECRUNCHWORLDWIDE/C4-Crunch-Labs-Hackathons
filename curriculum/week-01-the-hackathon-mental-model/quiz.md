# Week 1 — Quiz

Ten multiple-choice questions. Take it with your lecture notes closed. Aim for 9/10 before moving to Week 2. Answer key at the bottom — don't peek.

---

**Q1.** According to Lecture 1, which of the following best summarizes what hackathons actually reward?

- A) The most novel and ambitious idea, regardless of execution.
- B) Problem clarity, technical depth, demo-ability, and polish — roughly equally.
- C) Whichever team has the most senior engineers.
- D) Whoever uses the most sponsor APIs in one project.

---

**Q2.** The **12-hour rule** says that at the 12-hour mark of a 36-hour event:

- A) The whole team must have slept at least 4 hours.
- B) The remaining scope must fit in the remaining time, plus a 30% buffer, or you cut.
- C) The pitch lead must have written the script.
- D) All sponsor APIs must be integrated.

---

**Q3.** The **demo-or-die doctrine** says:

- A) If you cannot demo your project live, you have to record it instead.
- B) If a feature does not appear in the three-minute demo, it does not exist in the judges' eyes.
- C) Your demo must be at least three minutes long or it will not be judged.
- D) You should always start with the demo and then write the README.

---

**Q4.** In a MoSCoW chart, what does the **W** stand for, and why does the C4 voice say it is the most important column?

- A) "Will" — because it lists what you will build.
- B) "Want" — because it lists what users want.
- C) "Won't" — because explicitly listing what you will not build is the dominant scoping skill.
- D) "Wait" — because it lists what to do after the event.

---

**Q5.** Which of the following is the **only non-optional** canonical role on a hackathon team?

- A) Pitch lead.
- B) Designer.
- C) Full-stack builder.
- D) PM / scope-keeper.

---

**Q6.** A team has five MUSTs in their MoSCoW chart at hour zero. According to the C4 voice, the most likely problem is:

- A) The team is too small — they need more builders.
- B) Five MUSTs is two musts too many; the team is describing two projects, not one.
- C) They need to add more SHOULDs to balance the chart.
- D) Five is fine, since they have 36 hours to build.

---

**Q7.** Which is the most accurate statement about sponsor prizes?

- A) They are corporate fluff; serious teams ignore them.
- B) They are usually easier to win than the grand prize because the pool of competing teams is smaller, but forcing a sponsor tool into a project that does not fit will show.
- C) A team should stack as many sponsor APIs as possible to maximize prize chances.
- D) Sponsor prizes are only awarded at virtual hackathons.

---

**Q8.** When should the team's deploy URL first go live (according to the team-contract template in Exercise 3)?

- A) Hour 2 — with a blank page if needed. All feature work after that targets the deploy URL.
- B) Hour 18 — once the backend is stable.
- C) Hour 30 — once the project is feature-complete.
- D) Hour T-1 — one hour before submission.

---

**Q9.** A team is at hour 33 of a 36-hour event. What does the C4 doctrine say should be happening?

- A) Aggressively adding new features to close the gap with other teams.
- B) The final-3-hour freeze: no new features, only demo rehearsal, recording, and submission polish.
- C) Pulling an all-nighter to finish the wishlist.
- D) Switching frameworks if the current one is too slow.

---

**Q10.** Which sentence best matches the C4 voice for a post-mortem?

- A) "Pat broke the rate limit and cost us the win."
- B) "The team underperformed because not everyone tried hard enough."
- C) "The sprint board did not catch the API rate limit, and the team had no fallback plan for that."
- D) "Our project was the most innovative, but the judges did not understand it."

---

## Answer key

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

1. **B** — Problem clarity, technical depth, demo-ability, and polish, roughly equally weighted. "Novelty" is implicit at best. (Lecture 1, §1)
2. **B** — The 12-hour rule formally: remaining scope ≤ remaining time × 0.7. The 30% buffer absorbs the surprises every event produces. (Lecture 2, §3)
3. **B** — If it does not appear in the three-minute demo, it does not exist. Build only what can demo; cut everything else. (Lecture 2, §4)
4. **C** — "Won't." Explicitly naming what you will not build is the most important scoping skill. An implied WON'T is a WON'T that comes back at hour 28. (Lecture 2, §2)
5. **D** — PM / scope-keeper. A team without a scope-keeper builds a wishlist and ships zero of it. (Lecture 1, §3)
6. **B** — MUSTs cap at three. Five MUSTs means the project is really two projects. (Lecture 2, §2)
7. **B** — Sponsor prizes are usually easier to win because of smaller team pools, but forcing a sponsor's tool into a project that does not fit visibly hurts. (Lecture 1, §5)
8. **A** — Hour 2, with a blank page if needed. All feature work targets the deploy URL after that. (Lecture 2, §5; Exercise 3, §7)
9. **B** — The final-3-hour freeze: rehearsal, recording, submission polish, no new features. (Lecture 2, §7)
10. **C** — Blameless language. The sprint board did not catch it; the team had no fallback. Not "Pat broke it" and not "the judges did not understand." (Lecture 2, §6; brand book, §Voice rules)

</details>

# Week 10 — Post-Event Carry-Out and Organizing Your Own

Welcome to **Week 10** of **C4 · Crunch Labs Hackathons**. This is the final week of the track. Week 9 took the team's submission into the judging room and rehearsed the four minutes that decide the score. Week 10 takes the team *back out of the room* and answers the two questions that decide whether the hackathon was worth the weekend: *what did the team do with the project after the event closed*, and *can the team now run an event of its own*. Both halves of the week are deliverable; both halves are graded against checklists; both halves are real-world, recruiter-grade, and reusable.

Week 10 is the *carry-out and stand-up* layer of the C4 stack. The build was Week 3. The contract was Week 4. The scope was Week 5. The script was Week 6. The first 12 hours of the team-mode run was Week 7. The closing was Week 8. The judging was Week 9. This week is the seven days *after* the closing ceremony. What the team does with those seven days separates the hackathons that recruiters notice from the hackathons that quietly disappear into a private GitHub repo with no README.

The deliverable is a **committed `POST-EVENT-LOG.md`** in the same repo as `DAY-1-LOG.md`, `DAY-2-LOG.md`, and `JUDGE-ROOM-LOG.md` from previous weeks, plus a **`README-FOR-RECRUITERS.md`** at the repo root, plus a **90-second demo video** uploaded to YouTube (unlisted is fine), plus a **LinkedIn post draft** in `POST-EVENT-LOG.md`. The mini-project this week is the **capstone** for the whole track — an 8-week plan to organize a one-day campus hackathon, with venue, sponsorship outreach, MLH membership decision, insurance, food, code of conduct, judging panel, prize pool, sponsor liability, and a minute-by-minute day-of run-of-show. The capstone is the proof that the learner can not only *attend* hackathons but also *run* them — which is the seat most cohorts move into within 12 months of finishing C4.

Most learners reach Week 10 with three artifacts (the build, the demo video, the judge-room log) and zero plan for what to do with them. The default outcome is: the repo sits private; the demo video sits in Google Drive; the team disperses; and six months later, a recruiter asks "what have you built?" and the team scrambles to remember which repo and which video. Week 10 names that failure mode, prescribes the seven-day carry-out checklist that prevents it, and then doubles down — by teaching the learner the org-side skills (venue, sponsorship, insurance, run-of-show) that make the *next* hackathon possible. The hackathon ecosystem runs on a small number of people who know how to run events; C4 ends with the learner being one of those people.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

- **Convert** the team's hackathon submission into a recruiter-grade README — the named seven-section layout (problem, demo link, stack, how-it-runs, screenshots, team, license) — and commit it at the repo root as `README-FOR-RECRUITERS.md` so the existing build README does not need to be edited.
- **Record** a 90-second demo video on a phone — landscape orientation, one cut, captioned in-frame title card — using the four-beat phone-demo arc (problem 15s, demo 50s, stack 15s, ask 10s) and upload it as an unlisted YouTube video or a public Loom.
- **Write** a post-event blog or tweet thread using the C4 *anatomy of a hackathon retro* template — the five named beats (what we built, what worked, what broke, what we'd change, the next event we want to attend) — at the right length for the platform (Twitter thread: 5–7 tweets; Medium/Substack: 600–900 words; LinkedIn long-form: 1,200–1,500 words).
- **Add** the project to LinkedIn — the *Featured* section, the *Projects* section, and the *Experience* section if the hackathon was hosted by a recognised organisation — with the named phrasing patterns ("built X in 24 hours at Y") and the screenshot-plus-link convention.
- **Add** the project to the team's collective resumes — the *Projects* section, the named one-line-plus-bullets format, the verb-first bullet pattern, and the link-to-repo-plus-demo-video convention.
- **Decide** whether to organise a campus hackathon of your own — the four-question gating decision (do you have a venue, do you have at least three co-organisers, do you have at least eight weeks lead time, do you have any sponsor leads), and the C4 voice on when the answer is "not yet."
- **Scope** a one-day campus hackathon — the named six-track structure (date, venue, capacity, theme, prize pool, judging format), the budget ranges for a 50-person event versus a 150-person event, and the difference between a *learner-run* event and an *MLH member event*.
- **Apply** for **Major League Hacking membership** for learner events — the tier structure (Local, Member, Premier), the named benefits per tier (hardware lab, swag, judge introductions, insurance via MLH), the application timeline, and when each tier is worth it.
- **Source** sponsors using the C4 *cold-email template* — the seven-line structure (one-line intro, the event, the ask, the audience, the deliverables, the tier menu, the close), the named follow-up rhythm (day 0, day 7, day 14, drop), and the realistic conversion rate (3–8 percent).
- **Secure** insurance, food, and dietary coverage — the named insurance options (university-provided, MLH-provided, third-party event policy), the food-budget rule-of-thumb ($8–12 per attendee per meal in the US in 2026 dollars), the dietary checklist (vegetarian, vegan, gluten-free, halal, kosher, nut allergies), and the named *food fail modes* (under-ordering, no labels, no late-night refuel).
- **Publish** a code of conduct — adapt the **MLH Code of Conduct** or the **Berlin Code of Conduct**, with the named *incident response chain* (report channel, on-call organiser, escalation, post-incident debrief).
- **Recruit** a judging panel — the named *3-3-3 rule* (three industry judges, three faculty/staff judges, three alumni or learner judges), the cold-email pattern for judges, the timeline (judges confirmed 4 weeks out, briefed 1 week out), and the rubric pre-share.
- **Run** the day-of with a minute-by-minute run-of-show — the named segments (doors open, opening ceremony, hacking block 1, lunch, hacking block 2, dinner, hacking block 3, submissions close, judging, closing ceremony, teardown), the segment lengths for a one-day (8–10 hour) event versus a 24-hour event, and the named *staffing per segment*.
- **Carry out** the event after the closing — the named seven-day post-event checklist (thank-you emails to sponsors, judge thank-yous, photo gallery published, attendee survey sent, social-media wrap-up, financial reconciliation, archive the slack/discord), the named *post-event template doc*, and when to start planning the next one.

## Prerequisites

- **Week 9 completed.** `JUDGE-ROOM-LOG.md` is committed; the rubric self-score is in place; the mock pitch take notes are logged. Week 10 cannot start without the Week 9 artifacts because the carry-out reuses the team's own real submission, the mock-pitch take, and the rubric self-score.
- **Weeks 1 through 8 completed.** The Hackathon Operating Document, the template repo, the solo prototype, the team contract, the scoping worksheet, the three-minute solo demo recording, the team-mode dry-run logs, and the demo video URL. Every artifact from those eight weeks is reused in this week.
- **A phone with a camera and a microphone that works.** The 90-second phone demo is recorded on a phone, in landscape, single take. A laptop webcam works as a fallback but rehearses a different skill. Phones are where the audience watches; the recording medium matches the consumption medium.
- **A LinkedIn account.** If the learner does not have one, this week is the prompt to set one up. LinkedIn is where the project lives for recruiters; without an account, the recruiter-grade README has nowhere to point.
- **A read of two public *organizer guides*.** The MLH organizer guide and the NumFOCUS event playbook are the two anchor references. Both are free. Both are linked in `resources.md`.
- **(Optional but recommended) A meeting with one prospective co-organiser** before starting the mini-project. The capstone is *organising* an event, not *attending* one. Organising alone is hard; the capstone assumes the learner has at least a conversation with one peer about co-organising.

A reminder: the capstone is a **plan**, not a live event. The learner does not need to actually run a hackathon to pass Week 10. The deliverable is the 8-week plan document, the cold-email drafts, the run-of-show, the budget, the code of conduct adaptation, and the post-event template. Whether the learner then actually runs the event is a decision for after C4.

## Topics covered

- **The seven-day carry-out window.** What the team does in the seven days after the closing ceremony separates "this hackathon was the start of something" from "this hackathon was a weekend that disappeared." The carry-out checklist is the named C4 artifact for the seven days.
- **The recruiter-grade README.** Seven-section template: problem statement, demo link (video first, repo second), tech stack, how to run locally, screenshots, team and credits, license. The recruiter-grade README is a *separate file* from the build README so the build README can stay technical.
- **The 90-second phone demo.** Why 90 seconds and not 3 minutes. The phone-demo arc (problem 15s, demo 50s, stack 15s, ask 10s). The single-take rule. The captioned in-frame title card. The landscape-only rule. The upload destinations (YouTube unlisted, Loom public, never raw cloud storage).
- **The post-event blog/tweet anatomy.** Five named beats (what we built, what worked, what broke, what we'd change, the next event). Lengths per platform. The screenshot rule. The link rule. The credit rule.
- **LinkedIn and resume placement.** The *Featured* section convention. The *Projects* section format. The verb-first bullet pattern. The when-to-list-the-org-name pattern. The link-to-repo-plus-demo convention. The named *do not lie about prizes* rule.
- **The four-question gating decision.** Whether the learner should organise their own hackathon: venue, co-organisers, lead time, sponsor leads. Failing one of the four is fine; failing two or more is the C4 *"not yet"* signal.
- **The org-side six-track structure.** Date, venue, capacity, theme, prize pool, judging format. Each track has named gates and named failure modes.
- **Budget ranges for learner-run events.** 50-person event: $1,500–$4,000. 150-person event: $5,000–$15,000. The named *biggest line items* (food, swag, prizes, AV) and the *cuttable line items* (swag, branded swag, decorations).
- **MLH membership tiers.** Local, Member, Premier. What each unlocks (hardware lab, swag, judge intros, insurance, marketing reach). The application timeline (start 8–12 weeks before the event). The C4 voice on when to apply.
- **The cold-email template for sponsors.** Seven-line structure. Follow-up rhythm. Tier menu (Bronze/Silver/Gold/Title). Realistic conversion rate. The named *non-cash sponsorship asks* (API credits, swag, judges, mentors).
- **Insurance.** Three options: university-provided, MLH-provided (Member tier and up), third-party event policy. The named *minimum coverage* for a learner-run event. The named *who-signs* question.
- **Food and dietary.** Per-attendee budget rule-of-thumb. Meal cadence for one-day events versus 24-hour events. The dietary checklist (vegetarian, vegan, gluten-free, halal, kosher, nut allergies). The labeling rule. The late-night refuel rule.
- **Code of conduct.** Adapting the MLH or Berlin code. The incident response chain. The on-call organiser. The escalation path. The post-incident debrief.
- **The judging panel.** The 3-3-3 rule. The cold-email pattern for judges. The brief-the-judges meeting. The rubric pre-share. The thank-you sequence.
- **The prize pool.** Named structures: flat-prize, tracks-and-overall, sponsor-prizes-plus-overall. The named *no-cash-only* recommendation for learner events (cash creates tax and equity headaches). The named alternatives (gift cards, hardware, conference tickets, mentor sessions).
- **Sponsor liability.** What sponsors expect from the event in terms of signed agreements. The named *sponsorship deck*, *signed MOU*, and *post-event recap deliverables*. The named *brand-protection clauses*.
- **The day-of run-of-show.** Minute-by-minute schedule for a one-day campus event. Staffing per segment. The named *quiet-zone*, *check-in flow*, *swag table*, *signage placement*.
- **The seven-day post-event close-out.** Thank-you emails, judge thank-yous, photo gallery, attendee survey, social-media wrap-up, financial reconciliation, archive the chat. The named *post-event template doc*.
- **The track wrap-up sidebar.** Ten weeks of C4 in one paragraph each. What the learner can do now that the learner could not do ten weeks ago. The named *next-step branches* (run the next event, attend a regional, contribute to an open-source project, judge for another cohort).

## Stretch goals

If you finish the capstone on Sunday and want to push further, try any of the following. None substitute for the capstone — commit the 8-week plan first, every time.

- **Pre-fill the MLH membership application.** The Member-tier application form is public. Filling it out (without submitting) surfaces every gap in the 8-week plan in 30 minutes. The unsubmitted draft is itself a useful artifact.
- **Schedule a 20-minute coffee with the campus learner-org office.** Most universities have a learner-org advisor who has seen a dozen learner-run events. The 20-minute conversation about venue, insurance, and risk is the cheapest derisking the capstone can buy.
- **Draft an actual sponsor email and send it.** With the team's consent and the advisor's sign-off, send one of the three drafted emails to a real sponsor (a local company, a regional MLH-friendly sponsor like DigitalOcean or Twilio). The exercise rehearses the *anxiety of sending*, not just the *quality of writing*.
- **Read the public post-event recap of one large hackathon.** HackMIT, TreeHacks, PennApps, HackTX, BoilerMake, Hack the North — all publish public recaps. Pattern-match the structure: what they reported, what they did not report, what the recap was selling, who the recap was selling to.
- **Run a 30-minute teach-back with two C4 peers.** Walk them through the capstone. The teach-back surfaces every assumption the learner made silently. The peers' questions are the *audit* the capstone needs before the advisor sees it.
- **Re-read the team's `DAY-2-LOG.md` and the `JUDGE-ROOM-LOG.md`.** With the org-side lens from this week. The learner now sees the event from both sides — attendee and organiser. The deltas are where the next event's organising decisions live.

## Track wrap-up — what ten weeks of C4 gave you

A short paragraph per week. Re-read this when the next hackathon is on the horizon and the team is asking "are we ready."

- **Week 1 — The Hackathon Mental Model.** You learned the named C4 vocabulary — *the demo is the deliverable*, *scope down, ship up*, *the team contract before the build*. You wrote the Hackathon Operating Document. You stopped treating hackathons as "code as much as possible in 24 hours" and started treating them as *delivery exercises with a four-minute final*.
- **Week 2 — Rapid Prototyping Toolkit.** You set up the C4 template repo — git, linting, deploy, CI, a smoke test. You learned the named tools (the template, the deploy preview, the README scaffold). You internalised that *the toolkit is the floor*; the build sits on top of the floor.
- **Week 3 — Solo Prototype Sprint.** You shipped a solo hackathon-style project end-to-end. One person, one weekend, one demo. The first time the *full loop* — idea, build, demo, repo — was the learner's responsibility alone. The artifact is the proof-of-readiness for team mode.
- **Week 4 — Agile Ceremonies, Real World.** You learned the team contract. Roles, working hours, conflict-resolution, demo ownership. The contract is the document the team signs *before* the build; the document is the predictor of whether the team will finish.
- **Week 5 — Scoping Discipline.** You learned the scoping worksheet. The named *must-have / nice-to-have / out-of-scope* layering. The named *kill list*. The scope is the difference between *we built something* and *we built something demo-able in the time we had*.
- **Week 6 — Pitch Craft.** You learned the 30-90-45-15 ratio. You recorded a three-minute solo demo. The script became muscle; the script became the muscle the team mode would later borrow.
- **Week 7 — Team Mode Dry-Run, Day One.** You ran the first 12 hours of a 24-hour event in team mode. You wrote `DAY-1-LOG.md`. The kickoff, the contract sign, the scope, the first build push, the demo plan.
- **Week 8 — Team Mode Dry-Run, Day Two.** You ran the closing 12 hours. You wrote `DAY-2-LOG.md`. The submission package. The demo video. The retro. The follow-up issue. The team shipped.
- **Week 9 — Judging, Scoring, and Reading the Room.** You learned the five rubric dimensions. You self-scored the team's submission. You rehearsed the mock pitch. You wrote `JUDGE-ROOM-LOG.md`. The judging is the *delivery layer*; you delivered.
- **Week 10 — Post-Event Carry-Out and Organising Your Own.** You wrote the recruiter-grade README. You recorded the 90-second phone demo. You drafted the LinkedIn post. You wrote the capstone — the 8-week plan to organise a campus hackathon, with budget, run-of-show, code of conduct, and post-event template. You are now on the *other side* of the table.

What you can do now that you could not do ten weeks ago:

1. **Read a hackathon brief and immediately scope it** to a 24-hour build, with a named must-have/nice-to-have split.
2. **Sign a team contract in writing** before the first commit, with named roles and named conflict-resolution moves.
3. **Record a three-minute pitch** in the 30-90-45-15 ratio without re-reading the script live.
4. **Anticipate the five most-asked judge questions** for a build and answer each in 30 seconds.
5. **Convert the build into a recruiter-grade README** with the seven-section template.
6. **Record a 90-second phone demo** in landscape, single take.
7. **Draft a sponsor cold-email** with the seven-line structure and a follow-up rhythm.
8. **Run a one-day campus hackathon** with a minute-by-minute run-of-show, a code of conduct, a judging panel, and a budget.
9. **Be a judge** on a friendly cohort or peer event using the five-dimension rubric.
10. **Carry out the seven-day post-event close-out** with thank-yous, survey, photo gallery, and financial reconciliation.

The track is over; the work is not. The next branches:

- **Run the campus event.** Take the 8-week plan, recruit two co-organisers, and run it. If the answer to all four gating questions is yes, the *start date* is the only decision left.
- **Attend a regional or national hackathon.** HackMIT, TreeHacks, PennApps, BoilerMake, Hack the North, AthenaHacks, HackHarvard, MHacks. You have the toolkit. Apply.
- **Contribute to an open-source project on a sustained cadence.** The hackathon repo is open; keep it open. File one issue a week; merge one PR a month. The repo becomes the year-round portfolio that the hackathon weekend started.
- **Judge for another cohort.** C4 cohorts swap judges at year-end events. The first time you judge, you'll notice every Lecture-3 cue you learned in Week 9 from the *other seat*.
- **Mentor the next C4 cohort.** The fastest way to internalise the ten weeks is to teach them. Volunteer as a Week-3-onwards mentor for the next intake.

## The named C4 alumni paths after Week 10

C4 alumni surveys (cohort years 2019–2025) cluster the post-track paths into five named branches. Each branch is valid; the cohort distributes across all five.

- **Path A — The Repeat Organiser.** Approximately 22 percent of alumni run the capstone live within 12 months. The capstone document is the operating spec for the event. The named *year-two return* is the strongest indicator of long-term hackathon-organising leadership; a second event run by the same team predicts a third, fourth, and beyond.
- **Path B — The Regional or National Attendee.** Approximately 35 percent of alumni apply to a regional or national hackathon (HackMIT, TreeHacks, PennApps, BoilerMake, Hack the North, AthenaHacks, HackHarvard, MHacks) within 12 months. The Week 10 toolkit — recruiter-grade README, 90-second phone demo, LinkedIn placement — directly feeds the *team-formation* signal for these events.
- **Path C — The Open-Source Contributor.** Approximately 18 percent of alumni take the hackathon repo public and maintain it as a sustained open-source project. The named *one issue a week, one PR a month* cadence (Lecture 1 §1 stretch) is the floor; some alumni maintain 5x that pace. The repo grows into a year-round portfolio that the hackathon weekend started.
- **Path D — The Judge or Mentor.** Approximately 15 percent of alumni judge or mentor for a cohort or peer event within 12 months. The named seat-swap (from attendee to judge) sharpens the *grader's eye* and feeds back into the alumnus's own future submissions.
- **Path E — The Curriculum Contributor.** Approximately 10 percent of alumni return as a mentor or co-instructor for the next C4 intake. Teaching is the fastest internalisation; mentors typically report that *teaching Week 5 (scoping) and Week 6 (pitch craft)* surfaces gaps the alumnus had not noticed in their own original cohort run.

The named distribution does not sum to 100 percent — many alumni take multiple paths concurrently. The C4 voice is that all five paths are reinforcing; the alumnus who runs the campus event (A) is also more likely to mentor the next cohort (E) and to apply to a regional with the campus team (B).

## What you should *not* do after Week 10

A counter-list, equally important. The C4 voice on each:

- **Do not let the recruiter-grade README rot.** Update it whenever the project ships a meaningful change. The README's *last-updated* date is what recruiters check after the 90-second video.
- **Do not commit the team's hackathon credentials in `.env`.** The named *post-event security pass* is to rotate any API keys that were committed during the rush. Twilio sandbox keys, OpenAI keys, Plaid sandbox credentials — rotate all of them in the first 7 days.
- **Do not block the team from re-using the project.** The license decision in the README's Section 7 is binding. *All rights reserved* means a teammate cannot fork their own work for an internship interview unless the rest of the team agrees. Prefer MIT or Apache-2.0 unless there is a specific reason not to.
- **Do not over-promise on the next event.** If the four-question gate (Lecture 2 §1) fails two or more, the capstone plan is *not yet* to be executed. The *not yet* call is honest, not defeatist.
- **Do not skip the carry-out to start the capstone.** The carry-out (Lecture 1) and the capstone (Lectures 2–3) are not interchangeable. The carry-out is the team's individual portfolio; the capstone is the learner's organising plan. Both ship in Week 10.

# Exercise 3 — Draft Three Sponsor Cold-Emails

## Goal

Draft three distinct sponsor cold-emails for the capstone (mini-project) hackathon. Each email uses the C4 seven-line template from Lecture 3 §1, addresses a different *type* of sponsor (a large-tech sponsor, a regional or local company, an open-source-foundation or non-profit), and proposes a different tier. The exercise is the rehearsal for the capstone's sponsorship outreach; the three emails are the templates the learner will adapt 30–50 times during the real runway.

## Time-box

60–90 minutes total — 20–30 minutes per email.

## Pre-flight

- The capstone's draft event details (name, date, venue, attendee count, theme) — these are decided in the mini-project; for this exercise, use the working numbers from your draft and refine later.
- A list of three real candidate sponsors. The learner is *not* sending these emails; the exercise is to draft as if for a real ask, which improves rigor.
- The seven-line template from Lecture 3 §1 open in another tab.
- The tier menu and non-cash menu from Lecture 3 §1 open in another tab.

## Instructions

### Step 1 — Pick three sponsor types

Pick one of each:

- **Large-tech sponsor.** Examples: Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta, Apple, Stripe, GitHub, Atlassian, Salesforce, IBM, Oracle. These sponsors have *student outreach budgets* but slow procurement. Often a Gold-tier ask.
- **Regional or local company.** Examples for a Miami-area event: Magic Leap, Citrix (Fort Lauderdale), JPMorgan Chase Innovation Lab (Tampa), Carnival Cruise Line tech division, NextEra Energy, local consulting firms. These sponsors decide faster and have smaller budgets. Often a Silver-tier or Bronze-tier ask.
- **Open-source foundation or non-profit.** Examples: Linux Foundation, Apache Foundation, Python Software Foundation, NumFOCUS, Django Foundation, Cloud Native Computing Foundation, Mozilla Foundation, GitHub Education, DigitalOcean. These sponsors often provide *non-cash* (credits, swag, judges) rather than cash. Often a Bronze-tier or non-cash equivalent.

### Step 2 — Draft Email 1 (Large-tech sponsor, Gold-tier ask)

Use the seven-line template:

```
Subject: <Event Name> sponsorship — <University>, <Date>

Hi <First Name>,

Line 1 — One-line intro. Who you are.
Line 2 — The event (one sentence — theme, date, attendees, venue).
Line 3 — The ask (Gold-tier, $2,000–$5,000, named deliverables).
Line 4 — The audience (majors, year, geographic draw, recruiting overlap).
Line 5 — The Gold-tier deliverables (logo, branded table, speaking slot,
         sponsor-named prize, recruiter table).
Line 6 — The tier menu link (placeholder OK for now).
Line 7 — The close (20-minute call this week, or yes/no by date).

Best,
<Your Name>
<Role>, <Event Name>
<Email> · <LinkedIn>
```

Fill it out for the large-tech sponsor. Target length: 180–260 words in the body (subject + signature don't count).

Save as `mini-project/sponsorship/email-01-large-tech.md`.

### Step 3 — Draft Email 2 (Regional/local company, Silver-tier ask)

Same template, different sponsor, *different ask*. Silver-tier ($750–$1,500) is the smaller-budget tier. The Silver-tier deliverables are the named *branded table, swag distribution, logo placement* — no speaking slot, no sponsor-named prize.

Adjust:

- Line 3 — Silver-tier dollar range.
- Line 4 — Emphasise the *local* angle (geographic draw matches the sponsor's hiring pool).
- Line 5 — Silver-tier deliverables.
- Line 7 — A shorter call window ("10-minute call this week") because regional sponsors decide faster.

Save as `mini-project/sponsorship/email-02-regional.md`.

### Step 4 — Draft Email 3 (Open-source foundation or non-profit, non-cash ask)

Same template, but the ask is *non-cash*. Examples:

- API credits ($500–$5,000 in credit value).
- Swag (T-shirts, stickers, hardware kits).
- A judge from the foundation's engineering team.
- A workshop slot featuring the foundation's flagship project.

Adjust:

- Line 3 — Non-cash ask, named explicitly ("we're inviting <Foundation> to contribute $X in API credits plus one judge").
- Line 5 — The non-cash deliverables to the foundation ("logo placement, mention of the credits at opening ceremony, recap deck within 14 days").
- Line 7 — The close adjusted for foundations' slower internal cycles ("a yes/no by <date 4 weeks out>").

Save as `mini-project/sponsorship/email-03-foundation.md`.

### Step 5 — Cross-review

Read all three emails back. Confirm:

- Each one is a different sponsor type.
- Each one names a different tier or non-cash ask.
- All three open with a one-line intro and end with a specific close.
- None of them exceeds 280 words in the body.
- All three would survive a 30-second skim by a sponsorship rep.

## Acceptance criteria

- [ ] Three emails drafted and saved in `mini-project/sponsorship/`.
- [ ] Each email uses the seven-line template.
- [ ] Email 1 targets a large-tech sponsor at Gold tier.
- [ ] Email 2 targets a regional/local company at Silver tier.
- [ ] Email 3 targets an open-source foundation or non-profit with a non-cash ask.
- [ ] Each email has a specific subject line, a specific event description, a specific ask, a specific audience description, a specific deliverables list, a tier menu link (placeholder OK), and a specific close.
- [ ] Each email body is between 120 and 280 words.
- [ ] No filler adjectives ("amazing event," "incredible opportunity," "world-class talent"). Replace each with a *specific* fact.

## Common pitfalls

- **Vague audience description.** "Talented engineering students" is vague. "60% CS, 25% IT, 15% design; 70% second-or-third-year undergrads" is specific.
- **Vague event description.** "A hackathon at FIU" is vague. "A one-day civic-tech hackathon on October 18 at FIU's Engineering Atrium 200, hosting 80 students" is specific.
- **No close.** Email ends with "Thanks!" with no action. The close is *the* line that determines reply rate.
- **Asks for everything.** "We'd love any support you can offer" is the named *no-conversion close*. Pick one tier; ask for that tier.
- **All three emails identical.** The exercise is to write *three differentiated* drafts. If the only changes between the three are the sponsor name, redo.

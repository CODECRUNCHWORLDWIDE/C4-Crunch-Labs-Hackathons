# Lecture 3 — Day-of: Judging Panel, Prize Pool, Sponsor Liability, Run-of-Show

## Why this lecture closes the track

Lecture 2 covered the eight-week paperwork runway: venue, sponsors, MLH, insurance, food, code of conduct. This lecture covers the last four weeks and the day-of: the judging panel, the prize pool, the sponsor liability paperwork, the minute-by-minute run-of-show, the seven-day post-event close-out, and the named *track wrap-up sidebar* that frames what the learner can do at the end of ten weeks of C4. This lecture is the *operational* sibling of Lecture 2's *contractual* work. By the time the learner closes this lecture, the capstone is ready to be drafted.

A note on tone: this lecture is *checklists, scripts, and templates*. The C4 voice is that the day-of is *the cheapest part of the event to over-prepare for* and *the most expensive part of the event to under-prepare for*. The lecture density reflects that ratio.

## 1. The cold-email template for sponsors

Sponsorship is the financial backbone of a sponsored event. The C4 cold-email template runs through every section the lecture covers. It is shared here, in Lecture 3, because the sponsorship outreach starts at week 6 of the runway — the same time the judging panel, prize pool, and run-of-show are being scoped. The three tracks are interlocking.

### The seven-line structure

```text
Subject: [Event Name] sponsorship — [University], [Date]

Line 1 — One-line intro. Who you are, what you're organising.
Line 2 — The event. One sentence on theme, date, expected attendees, venue.
Line 3 — The ask. The named tier (Bronze/Silver/Gold/Title) and the
         dollar range or named non-cash equivalent.
Line 4 — The audience. Who the attendees are (majors, year, geographic
         draw). Why this audience matches the sponsor.
Line 5 — The deliverables. What the sponsor gets at each tier (logo
         placement, branded table, speaking slot, swag distribution).
Line 6 — The tier menu (link). Link to the sponsorship deck (Challenge 2
         this week) or the public tier menu page on the event website.
Line 7 — The close. A specific request: a 20-minute call this week,
         or a yes/no by a specific date.
```

### The named tier menu

Most student-event sponsorship menus run a four-tier structure. Exact dollar amounts vary by event size and region; the structure is stable.

- **Bronze.** $250–$500. Logo on the event website. Logo on the back of the event T-shirt (if there is one). Mention in the closing-ceremony slides. No on-site presence.
- **Silver.** $750–$1,500. Everything in Bronze. Plus: branded table at the event for 4–8 hours during the day. Plus: ability to distribute swag at registration.
- **Gold.** $2,000–$5,000. Everything in Silver. Plus: a 5-minute opening-ceremony speaking slot. Plus: a sponsor-branded prize ($300–$500 prize value for a sponsor-named track). Plus: a recruiter table for the full event with two recruiter representatives.
- **Title.** $7,500+. Everything in Gold. Plus: the event is *co-branded* with the sponsor in marketing materials. Plus: a 15-minute opening-ceremony keynote slot. Plus: the sponsor names the *grand prize* and presents it at the closing ceremony. Plus: post-event recap deck delivered to the sponsor within 14 days.

### Non-cash asks

The C4 voice is *do not ask only for cash*. Many sponsors cannot route cash to student events but can route credits, hardware, or judges. The named non-cash menu:

- **API credits.** AWS, Google Cloud, DigitalOcean, Twilio, Stripe, Auth0, MongoDB Atlas, Vercel, Netlify. Every team gets a credit code; the sponsor's logo runs in the cred-redemption email.
- **Swag.** Stickers, T-shirts, mugs, water bottles. Cheap to ship in volume; high visual presence at the event.
- **Judges and mentors.** One or two engineers from the sponsor company, attending the event for the judging round and/or as mentors during the hacking block.
- **Workshop slot.** A 30–45 minute technical workshop during the hacking block (often early in Day 1) that demos the sponsor's API. Attendance is optional; the workshop slot is a soft sell.
- **Prize hardware.** A piece of hardware (a tablet, a development kit, a smart-home device) bundled into a prize track.

### The follow-up rhythm

- **Day 0.** Initial cold email.
- **Day 7.** First follow-up. One paragraph. Re-state the event, re-state the ask, ask for a yes/no by a specific date.
- **Day 14.** Second follow-up. One paragraph. Mention any other confirmed sponsors (social proof). Ask for a 10-minute call.
- **Day 21.** Drop. Do not send a fourth email. Mark the sponsor as a lead for next year.

### The named conversion rate

C4 cohort data: cold sponsorship outreach converts at **3–8 percent** for first-event teams without warm intros. Warm intros (a faculty member, an alumna at the company, a past organiser) convert at **15–25 percent**. The implication: to land 4 sponsors at $1,500 each, the team sends 50–130 cold emails or 16–28 warm-intro emails. Plan the outreach volume to the conversion floor.

### A sample cold email

```text
Subject: HackFIU 2026 sponsorship — Florida International University, Oct 18

Hi <First Name>,

I'm <Your Name>, co-organiser of HackFIU 2026, a one-day student
hackathon on October 18 at Florida International University.

We're hosting 80 undergraduate CS, IT, and design students for a 10-hour
build day, with a civic-tech theme. Doors open 9am; demos at 6pm.

We're inviting <Company Name> to join as a Silver-tier sponsor ($1,000),
which includes a branded table during the event, swag distribution at
registration, and logo placement on our website and event T-shirt.

The audience skews 60% CS, 25% IT, 15% design; 70% are 2nd-or-3rd-year
undergraduates. We see strong overlap with <Company Name>'s campus
recruiting funnel based on your recent JobVite posting for student
software engineering interns.

The full tier menu is in the linked deck:
<link to sponsorship deck PDF>

Could we hold a 20-minute call next Tuesday or Wednesday afternoon to
walk you through the deck and confirm fit?

Best,
<Your Name>
<Role>, HackFIU 2026
<Email> · <LinkedIn>
```

The email is *six short paragraphs*, *zero adjectives that strain credulity*, *one specific ask*, *one specific time window for the call*. Sponsorship reps read 50 cold emails a day; the email above survives the skim.

## 2. The judging panel — the 3-3-3 rule

The named C4 *3-3-3 rule* for judging-panel recruitment: aim for nine judges across three named cohorts of three each.

- **Three industry judges.** Engineers, designers, or product managers at local or regional tech companies. The sponsor-pipeline overlap is real; a Gold-tier sponsor frequently sends one of these three. Industry judges bring the *engineer* mindset (Lecture 9, the engineer-vs-investor distinction).
- **Three faculty / staff judges.** Professors or staff in the CS / Engineering / Design departments. Faculty bring the *long-arc* perspective; faculty also legitimise the event in the campus eyes and unlock future-year support.
- **Three alumni or student judges.** Recent graduates of the same university now working in industry, or senior students from a peer university (a returning C4 cohort, a TreeHacks-alumni network, a regional MLH connection). Alumni bring the *bridge* perspective — they remember being attendees and they speak to attendees in attendee-language.

### Why nine

Nine judges, in a *table-walk format* (Lecture 9 §6), can each judge approximately 6–9 tables in a 90-minute judging window. That gives every team approximately *3 judge visits*, which is the named *floor for fair scoring*. Fewer than three judge visits per team means the score is highly volatile; more than three is over-engineering for a first event.

For a *panel format* (single seated panel, 5–7 minutes per team), five to seven judges is sufficient; the 3-3-3 rule reduces to a *2-2-2* shape (six judges).

### The cold-email pattern for judges

```text
Subject: Judge invitation — HackFIU 2026, Oct 18 (4:30–6:30pm)

Hi <First Name>,

I'm <Your Name>, co-organiser of HackFIU 2026, a one-day student
hackathon on October 18 at Florida International University.

I'd like to invite you to be a judge in our 4:30–6:30pm judging window.
You'd score approximately 8 student teams against a five-dimension
rubric (we'll share the rubric one week in advance). The commitment is
2 hours on-site plus a 30-minute pre-event briefing call the week before.

Why we're asking you specifically: <one sentence on the personal angle —
their domain expertise, their connection to the theme, their past role
as a judge at a related event>.

Could you confirm by <date 3 weeks out>?

Best,
<Your Name>
```

The email is *four short paragraphs*. The personal angle in paragraph three is the move that gets the *yes*. Judges receive many invitations; the named *why-you-specifically* line is the differentiator.

### Timeline

- **8 weeks out.** First wave of judge invitations (industry + faculty).
- **6 weeks out.** Second wave (alumni + student).
- **4 weeks out.** All judges confirmed; calendar holds in writing.
- **2 weeks out.** Brief-the-judges meeting (30-minute video call). Rubric shared. Judging format walked through. Day-of schedule walked through. Questions answered.
- **1 week out.** Reminder email with the rubric and the day-of schedule. Re-confirm attendance.
- **Day-of.** Judges arrive 30 minutes before the judging window. Final brief (10 minutes). Then the judging window starts.

### The rubric pre-share

The named C4 voice: *judges score better when the rubric is in their hands a week before the event*. The rubric (the five-dimension Week 9 rubric, adapted) is shared in the brief-the-judges email. Judges who arrive cold to a rubric they have not seen score more variably; judges who arrive having read the rubric score with the dimensions in mind.

### Thank-you sequence

- **Day 0 (event day, after judging closes).** In-person thank-you. Hand them a branded thank-you note or a small gift (a coffee gift card, a sponsor-provided book).
- **Day 1 (next day).** Email thank-you, one paragraph, with a link to the post-event blog or the photo gallery.
- **Day 14 (post-event recap).** Recap email, including the team standings and the event photos. Asks if they'd be open to judging again next year.
- **Year + 1.** First-mover advantage on the next year's invitation.

## 3. The prize pool — three named structures

The prize pool is the named *visible incentive*. Most attendees do not attend for the prize, but the prize structure shapes the team formation, the project scoping, and the demo pitch. Three named structures, each with named tradeoffs.

### Structure A — Flat prize

- **What it is.** One overall prize for the best project. One winner. Often $500–$2,000 in value.
- **Pros.** Simple to communicate. Simple to judge (no track allocation). Fits a small (sub-40) event.
- **Cons.** *One* team wins; the *other* teams get nothing. Energy at the closing ceremony drops.
- **When to use.** Small first events (under 40 attendees, under 10 teams). Events where the team is short on prize budget.

### Structure B — Tracks and overall

- **What it is.** An overall prize plus 3–5 track prizes. Tracks are theme-aligned (Civic Tech, Education, Climate, Health, Open). Each track has its own winner; the overall winner is picked from the track winners.
- **Pros.** Multiple winning teams; the closing ceremony has multiple wins to celebrate. Easier to recruit thematic sponsors (a sponsor names a track).
- **Cons.** More judging complexity (track judges, overall judges). More prize-pool budget.
- **When to use.** Medium first events (40–100 attendees, 10–25 teams). The default named C4 recommendation.

### Structure C — Sponsor prizes plus overall

- **What it is.** An overall prize plus *sponsor-named* prizes. Each Gold-tier sponsor names and funds one prize ($300–$500 typically). The sponsor's representatives judge their own track.
- **Pros.** Sponsorship revenue funds the prize pool. Each sponsor gets a named outcome at the closing ceremony.
- **Cons.** Coordination overhead. Sponsor judges must be briefed on the C4 *do not coach* rule from Week 9.
- **When to use.** Larger events (100+ attendees) with a strong sponsor pipeline.

### The named no-cash-only recommendation

The C4 voice on student-event prizes: *prefer hardware, gift cards, or experiences over cash*. Reasons:

- **Tax.** Cash prizes over a low threshold (often $600 in the US) trigger 1099 reporting requirements; the event must collect tax info from winners and file with the IRS. Student events without a treasurer who knows tax law should not be touching this.
- **Equity.** Cash prizes split unevenly among team members create disputes. Hardware (a phone, a tablet, a development kit) is *less divisible*; a $500 development kit is "the team's" in a way that $500 in cash is not.
- **Visible value.** A $500 piece of hardware on a desk is visible for years; a $500 cash prize is invisible the day after.

Named cash-equivalent menu:

- **Gift cards.** Amazon, Apple, Best Buy. The $500 limit is the named *do-not-cross* line before the 1099 question matters.
- **Hardware.** Tablets, laptops (rare for a first event budget), VR headsets, drones, smart-home devices, development kits (Raspberry Pi, Arduino starter kits, ESP32 boards). Hardware sourced via sponsor donation reduces budget pressure.
- **Conference tickets.** Pass to a regional conference (PyCon, RustConf, SIGGRAPH-Student, GHC, AfroTech). Plus travel stipend if the budget allows.
- **Mentor sessions.** Three 1-hour 1:1 mentor sessions with a sponsor's senior engineer. Soft prize, high reputation, zero cost (the sponsor donates the time).

## 4. Sponsor liability and the signed paperwork

Sponsorship is *not* a handshake. The C4 voice is firm: *every sponsor commitment is in writing*. Three named artifacts:

### Artifact A — The sponsorship deck (PDF)

5–8 slides. Slide 1: event one-pager (date, venue, attendee count, theme). Slide 2: audience demographics (majors, year, gender split, geographic draw). Slide 3: the tier menu (Bronze/Silver/Gold/Title with named deliverables). Slide 4: past-event recap (or peer-event recap if there is no past event). Slide 5: sponsor logo placement examples (mock-ups). Slide 6: the ask (one sentence, one call-to-action). Slide 7: organising team and contact. Slide 8: appendix (code of conduct, insurance summary, MLH affiliation).

Challenge 2 this week walks the learner through drafting the deck.

### Artifact B — The signed MOU (memorandum of understanding)

A one-page MOU signed by the sponsor and the lead organiser, listing:

- The sponsor's tier.
- The named deliverables the event commits to (logo placement, branded table, speaking slot, swag distribution).
- The named deliverables the sponsor commits to (cash amount, non-cash items, headcount of judges/mentors).
- The payment timeline (often: 50 percent on signature, 50 percent within 30 days post-event upon delivery of the recap).
- The brand-protection clauses: the sponsor's logo is used only on materials approved by the sponsor; the event does not associate the sponsor with the code-of-conduct enforcement of attendees; the sponsor is not liable for any attendee injury, IP claim, or third-party loss arising from the event.
- The named *force majeure* clause: if the event is cancelled for reasons outside both parties' control (campus closure, public health order, severe weather), the sponsorship deposit is refunded *minus actual costs already paid by the event*.

The MOU is one page. Longer MOUs read as legalistic and intimidate first-time sponsors. The named one-page floor is the C4 voice.

### Artifact C — The post-event recap deliverable

Within 14 days of the event, the team sends the sponsor a *recap deck or recap doc* including: attendee headcount, project count, judging rubric and winners, photos of the sponsor's logo/table/speaking slot, the closing-ceremony slide with the sponsor's name visible, and the sponsor's team's photos at the event. The recap is what the sponsor's marketing team uses to *justify the spend internally*; without the recap, the sponsor is unlikely to renew the next year.

## 5. The day-of run-of-show (one-day campus event)

A minute-by-minute schedule for a one-day campus hackathon. The schedule is the *named C4 default*; events customise but rarely deviate from the block structure. Times are in 24-hour format.

| Segment                                  | Start | End   | Duration | Staffing                         |
|------------------------------------------|-------|-------|----------|----------------------------------|
| Organisers arrive, venue setup           | 07:30 | 08:30 | 60 min   | All organisers                   |
| Sponsor table setup, judge tech check    | 08:30 | 09:00 | 30 min   | Logistics + outreach leads       |
| Doors open, attendee check-in, breakfast | 09:00 | 09:30 | 30 min   | 2 check-in volunteers + breakfast volunteer |
| Opening ceremony (welcome, CoC recital, theme, judging walk-through, sponsor intros) | 09:30 | 10:15 | 45 min   | Lead organiser on mic            |
| Team formation (for attendees without a team)   | 10:15 | 10:45 | 30 min   | Programming lead facilitates     |
| Hacking block 1                          | 10:45 | 12:30 | 105 min  | All organisers floor-walk        |
| Sponsor workshop slot 1 (optional)       | 11:00 | 11:30 | 30 min   | Outreach lead introduces         |
| Lunch                                    | 12:30 | 13:30 | 60 min   | Food volunteer                   |
| Hacking block 2                          | 13:30 | 16:00 | 150 min  | All organisers floor-walk        |
| Afternoon snacks                         | 15:30 | 16:00 | 30 min   | Food volunteer                   |
| Hacking block 3 (sprint to submission)   | 16:00 | 17:30 | 90 min   | All organisers floor-walk        |
| Submissions close (Devpost or equivalent) | 17:30 | 17:30 | 0 min    | Lead organiser closes submissions |
| Demo prep + judging room setup           | 17:30 | 18:00 | 30 min   | Logistics lead                   |
| Judging window (table-walk format)       | 18:00 | 19:30 | 90 min   | Judges + lead organiser timekeeps |
| Light dinner (during judging)            | 18:00 | 19:00 | 60 min   | Food volunteer                   |
| Judges deliberate + closing-ceremony setup | 19:30 | 20:00 | 30 min   | Judges in private room           |
| Closing ceremony (winners + thank-yous + photo) | 20:00 | 20:45 | 45 min   | Lead organiser on mic            |
| Attendee farewell + venue teardown       | 20:45 | 21:30 | 45 min   | All organisers + volunteers      |
| Organisers' debrief over food            | 21:30 | 22:30 | 60 min   | All organisers                   |

### Named segment-specific notes

- **Opening ceremony (45 min).** Welcome (5 min). Code of conduct recital (3 min). Theme + judging rubric (10 min). Sponsor intros (12 min — 2 min per sponsor for up to 6 sponsors). Logistics walk-through (food, restrooms, on-call organiser, WiFi password) (10 min). Q&A (5 min).
- **Hacking blocks (total 345 min ≈ 5h45m).** Three blocks split by lunch and snacks. Block 1 is *team formation and scoping*; block 2 is *the build*; block 3 is *the sprint to submission*. Organisers floor-walk every 30 minutes — *visible presence* matters more than constant interruption.
- **Sponsor workshop slot.** Optional. One slot, 30 minutes, early in block 1, so teams that want to use a sponsor's API have a head start.
- **Judging window (90 min).** Table-walk format. Nine judges, ~10–15 teams. Each judge visits ~6 teams (≈12 min per team including transit). Team gets ~3 judge visits.
- **Closing ceremony (45 min).** Thank-yous to sponsors and judges (10 min). Track winners announced (15 min). Overall winner announced (5 min). Group photo (5 min). Goodbye and pointer to the post-event blog/survey (10 min).

### Staffing per segment

- **Lead organiser.** On mic during opening and closing. Floor-walks during hacking. Timekeeps during judging.
- **Logistics lead.** Manages venue setup, AV, food coordination, teardown.
- **Outreach lead.** Sponsor liaison day-of. Introduces sponsor workshops and speaking slots.
- **Programming lead.** Team formation facilitation, judging coordination, code-of-conduct on-call.
- **Volunteers (2–4).** Check-in, food, signage, restroom monitoring, parking direction.

### The named *signage placement*

Signage at: the venue entrance (event name + arrow), the registration desk (check-in here), the food line (dietary labels), the restrooms, the quiet zone (a designated quiet space for attendees who need a break — the named *quiet-zone* is the C4 default), the on-call organiser's location, the sponsor tables, the judging tables. Signage is *cheap and load-bearing*; absent signage is the named *attendee-confusion failure mode*.

## 6. The seven-day post-event close-out

The named C4 *post-event template* — a checklist the team commits to a `POST-EVENT-CLOSE-OUT.md` file (separate from the per-team `POST-EVENT-LOG.md` from Lecture 1; this one is the *organisers'* close-out, not the *attendees'* carry-out).

```markdown
# Post-Event Close-Out — <Event Name>, <Date>

## Day 0 (event day, after teardown)
- [ ] Organisers' debrief held (60 min, over food)
- [ ] Cash and receipts collected from each lead

## Day 1
- [ ] Thank-you email to all sponsors (templated, one paragraph, photos linked)
- [ ] Thank-you email to all judges (templated, one paragraph, recap link teased)
- [ ] Thank-you email to all mentors
- [ ] Faculty/staff advisor thanked (separately, in person if possible)

## Day 2
- [ ] Attendee survey designed and sent (Challenge 1 this week walks the design)
- [ ] Photo gallery uploaded to event website / Google Drive shared link
- [ ] Closing-ceremony video (if recorded) uploaded to YouTube unlisted

## Day 3
- [ ] Social-media wrap-up post(s) drafted (Twitter thread, LinkedIn, Instagram if applicable)
- [ ] Hashtag review (count uses of event hashtag; quote 3 best attendee posts)

## Day 4
- [ ] Financial reconciliation: every line item against actuals
- [ ] Vendor invoices paid (caterer, AV rental, swag printer)
- [ ] Refunds processed (sponsor force-majeure refunds, if any)
- [ ] Final budget vs. actual saved to organisers' drive

## Day 5
- [ ] Sponsor recap deck drafted (Lecture 3 §4)
- [ ] Sponsor recap sent to all paid sponsors

## Day 6
- [ ] Attendee survey results compiled (response rate + top quotes + top issues)
- [ ] Post-event blog drafted (organisers' version; separate from the per-team versions)
- [ ] Post-event blog reviewed by all co-organisers

## Day 7
- [ ] Post-event blog published
- [ ] Slack / Discord archived (export the channel; pin a closing message)
- [ ] Organisers' lessons-learned doc updated (one paragraph per gate: venue, food, judging, sponsorship, code of conduct, day-of)
- [ ] Next-year planning kicked off: hold the date 12 months out, capture the lessons in a "carry forward" doc
```

The close-out is *seven days* because each section needs its own day for review and feedback. Compressing the close-out to one day is the named failure mode of *post-event burnout*; the team is exhausted from the event itself and produces under-quality close-out work. The C4 voice: *one task per day, every day, for seven days*.

## 7. The track wrap-up — what ten weeks of C4 gave you

This lecture closes Week 10 and closes C4. The track wrap-up sidebar is intentionally re-stated here (it also appears at the bottom of the Week 10 README, deliberately twice — once in the index of the README, once at the conclusion of the final lecture). The wrap-up names the ten-week arc and points forward.

- **Week 1 — Hackathon mental model.** You named the vocabulary. *The demo is the deliverable. Scope down, ship up. The team contract before the build.*
- **Week 2 — Rapid prototyping toolkit.** You set up the template repo. *The toolkit is the floor.*
- **Week 3 — Solo prototype sprint.** You shipped a solo build. The first time the full loop was on your shoulders alone.
- **Week 4 — Agile ceremonies, real world.** You wrote the team contract. The document the team signs *before* the build.
- **Week 5 — Scoping discipline.** You wrote the scoping worksheet. The *must-have / nice-to-have / out-of-scope* layering.
- **Week 6 — Pitch craft.** You learned the 30-90-45-15 ratio. You recorded a solo demo. The script became muscle.
- **Week 7 — Team mode dry-run, day one.** You ran the first 12 hours. `DAY-1-LOG.md` is in the repo.
- **Week 8 — Team mode dry-run, day two.** You ran the closing 12 hours. The team shipped. `DAY-2-LOG.md` and the submission package.
- **Week 9 — Judging, scoring, and reading the room.** You rehearsed the four minutes that decide the score. `JUDGE-ROOM-LOG.md`.
- **Week 10 — Post-event carry-out and organising your own.** The recruiter-grade README. The 90-second phone demo. The LinkedIn placement. The capstone — the 8-week plan to run a campus hackathon, with run-of-show, code of conduct, prize pool, sponsor outreach, and the post-event template.

You attended in Weeks 1–9. You can now *run* in Week 10. The hackathon ecosystem runs on a small number of people who know how to run events. C4 ends with you being one of those people.

The track is ten weeks. The work is years. You have the toolkit. Go.

## 8. The named C4 day-of failure modes

Each failure mode below has a named *recovery move*. The recovery moves are part of the run-of-show; they live in the printed-and-distributed day-of doc that every organiser carries.

- **Failure mode A — opening-ceremony runs long.** Scheduled 45 minutes, takes 70. The hacking block is cut to 75 minutes. Recovery: the lead organiser sets a hard 45-minute cap, with a co-organiser timekeeping with a visible clock. If a sponsor speaker runs long, the lead organiser pre-arranges a polite "we have to wrap to keep the team-formation slot on time" cue before the speaker takes the mic.
- **Failure mode B — WiFi fails during hacking block 1.** Recovery: campus IT named contact's mobile number is on the lead organiser's phone. The named *hotspot backup* is enumerated in the venue walkthrough checklist — at least 4 mobile hotspots distributed across the organising team in case the venue WiFi is unrecoverable.
- **Failure mode C — caterer delivers late.** Lunch arrives at 1:00 instead of 12:30. Hacking block 2 starts 30 minutes late and runs 30 minutes short. Recovery: the *named backup snacks pantry* — a stocked side table with bagels, fruit, granola bars, and water that the food volunteer keeps replenished. The pantry is the bridge while the main lunch arrives.
- **Failure mode D — a sponsor representative does not arrive.** A Gold-tier sponsor's recruiter was scheduled to staff a branded table; they no-show. Recovery: the outreach lead has the sponsor's primary contact phone number; a 9am call confirms or fills the slot. The branded table stays up either way (printed materials suffice for the day's logo placement); the *recruiter table* is the slot that fails if the person no-shows.
- **Failure mode E — a judge runs late.** Recovery: judges are briefed to arrive 30 minutes before the judging window. If a judge is late, the *named alternate judge* (one alumnus on standby) slots in. The 3-3-3 panel becomes a 3-3-2 panel; the team rotation absorbs the loss.
- **Failure mode F — a code-of-conduct incident is reported.** Recovery: the named on-call organiser receives the report. The on-call escalates to the faculty advisor if serious. The post-incident debrief is scheduled for within 48 hours. The named *do not improvise* rule: the response chain is on paper, in the lead organiser's binder, before doors open.
- **Failure mode G — submissions close, but several teams have not submitted.** Recovery: the lead organiser opens a *15-minute grace window* announced over the mic, then closes hard. The named *grace window* is the only deviation from the published schedule that the lead organiser is empowered to make; the 15-minute cap is firm.

## 9. The day-of organiser's binder

Every lead organiser carries a *binder* on day-of. The named contents (Lecture 3 §5 expanded):

1. The printed run-of-show.
2. The printed staffing schedule.
3. The printed code of conduct and the incident response chain.
4. The list of named backup phone numbers (caterer backup, campus IT, faculty advisor, venue contact, MLH coach if applicable, alternate judge).
5. The signed venue contract and the insurance certificate (in case anyone asks).
6. The signed sponsor MOUs (for verification of deliverables).
7. The judging rubric (in case a judge forgot theirs).
8. The closing-ceremony script.
9. A printed list of attendee names + dietary flags from the application form (for the food volunteer).
10. A blank notebook for *day-of issue capture* — the named *what went wrong* log that feeds the post-event debrief.

The binder is a physical artifact. Cloud-stored versions of the same docs are useful, but the venue WiFi might fail; the campus printer might be locked; the lead organiser might forget their laptop charger. A printed binder works without electricity, signal, or platform login. The named C4 voice: *print the binder*.

## 10. Post-event compounding

The named *compounding mechanic* of running a hackathon is the part most first-time organisers underestimate. The first event is hard; the second event is approximately 40 percent cheaper to organise (in hours) because:

- The venue contact is already a relationship.
- The faculty/staff advisor is already on record.
- The MLH application is already approved (tier upgrades are easier than initial applications).
- The sponsor list has a named *renewal rate* of approximately 45–65 percent for first-event sponsors who got a clean recap deck within 14 days.
- The judging panel has a named *return rate* of approximately 60–80 percent for first-event judges who got a 14-day thank-you with photos.
- The organising team has named *operational knowledge* (where the power outlets are, where the WiFi access points are, which caterer's pizza arrives hot, which signage placement works).
- The code-of-conduct adoption and the incident-response chain are already in place.
- The post-event close-out template has been *exercised* — the team knows which tasks took longer than expected.

The first event is the *most expensive event the team will run*. The second event is materially cheaper, more polished, and better-attended. By the third event, the team is running with reputational capital: judges are easier to recruit, sponsors are easier to close, attendees apply faster. The compounding is the reason the C4 voice is *run the first event even if it is small*.

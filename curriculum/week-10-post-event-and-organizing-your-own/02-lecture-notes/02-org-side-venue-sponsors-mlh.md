# Lecture 2 — Org-Side: Venue, Sponsors, MLH, Insurance, Food

## Why this lecture comes second

The first lecture closed the *attendee* loop — the README, the video, the blog, the resume. This lecture opens the *organiser* loop. The two halves of Week 10 are intentionally asymmetric: the attendee work is individual and finishes in a week; the organiser work is structural and takes eight weeks of lead time. The capstone (mini-project) is the *plan* for that eight-week stretch, not the live execution. This lecture supplies the named gates, named tiers, named templates, and named failure modes for each of the six tracks the capstone plan covers — date, venue, capacity, theme, prize pool, judging format — plus the four cross-cutting tracks of MLH membership, insurance, food, and code of conduct.

A note on tone: this lecture reads as a series of named *gates*. Each gate is a *decision*, not a *preference*. The C4 voice is that student-run events fail predictably; they fail at the same gates; the lecture below is the gate-by-gate failure catalogue from the MLH organizer guide, the NumFOCUS event playbook, and the cohort-private retros C4 has collected since 2019. Reading the lecture without skimming is the cheapest insurance the capstone buys.

## 1. The four-question gating decision

Before scoping a hackathon, answer these four questions. The C4 voice on this is *do not begin scoping until all four are answered honestly*.

1. **Do you have a venue?** A *specific* room, with a *specific* contact at the campus, with a *specific* date that the room is held. "We can probably get a room" is *no*. "We have an email from the campus events office holding Engineering Atrium 200 from 9am to 9pm on October 18" is *yes*. The venue is the gating constraint for every other decision; without a venue, the rest of the plan is hypothetical.
2. **Do you have at least three co-organisers?** Three is the floor. One organiser is *a person doing too much*. Two organisers is *a single point of disagreement away from no event*. Three organisers is the smallest team that *can absorb one drop-out, one sick day, and one disagreement*. Strong events have 5–7 organisers; floor is three. Named role hint: one *programming lead* (judging, theme, MLH), one *logistics lead* (venue, food, AV), one *outreach lead* (sponsors, marketing, attendee recruitment).
3. **Do you have at least eight weeks lead time?** Eight weeks is the floor for a small (50-person) event; twelve weeks for a medium (150-person) event; twenty weeks for a large (300+) event. Less than eight weeks means *no sponsorship cycle*, *no MLH membership application window*, *no judge recruitment runway*. Less than eight weeks does not mean *no event*; it means *no sponsored event with judges*. The C4 voice is to push the date back, not to compress the runway.
4. **Do you have any sponsor leads?** *Any*. A faculty member who has a friend at a local tech company. A past organiser who can warm-intro you to MLH's sponsor team. A campus office that can underwrite a piece of the budget. The named *cold-start sponsorship rate* (C4 cohort data) is 3–8 percent; without a single warm lead, the team is starting from zero on the lowest-converting channel.

**Failing one of the four.** Fix that one. Push the date back to acquire lead time. Recruit one more co-organiser. Find one warm-intro sponsor lead. The four-question gate is not unanimous; *one fixable failure* is fine.

**Failing two or more of the four.** The C4 voice is *not yet*. The team is not stuck; the team is *early*. Volunteer at an existing event instead. Mentor a C4 cohort. Judge for a peer event. The *not yet* signal is honest, not defeatist; running a hackathon under two gating failures is the named *predictable-failure path*.

## 2. The org-side six-track structure

Once the four-question gate is passed, the six-track scoping structure starts. Each track has a named gate.

### Track A — Date

- **Constraint.** Campus academic calendar (no overlap with finals, no overlap with major university events, no overlap with major peer hackathons within 200 miles).
- **Floor lead time.** 8 weeks. Less is rushing the venue and the sponsors.
- **Day-of-week.** Saturday for a one-day event. Saturday-Sunday for a 24-hour event. Friday-night-to-Sunday-afternoon for a weekend event. Weekday hackathons are not advisable for a campus first event.
- **Named gate.** *The date is held in writing by the venue contact.* Not a phone call; a written confirmation, even a Slack message saved as a screenshot, even an email reply with "yes, that date is held." Verbal holds disappear when the venue's calendar gets a higher-priority booking.

### Track B — Venue

- **Capacity rule-of-thumb.** Floor space for hacking: 12–15 square feet per attendee (US units). A 50-person event needs 600–750 sq ft of hacking floor. A 150-person event needs 1,800–2,250 sq ft.
- **Beyond floor.** Separate space for: opening ceremony / closing ceremony (theatre-style seating; not the hacking floor), food serving (separate from laptops; spillage), restrooms (at scale), an organisers' room (the team needs a private space).
- **Power.** Outlets per attendee: 1.5 (most attendees bring a laptop and a phone). Power strips: budget for 1 per 4 attendees if outlets are sparse. Named *power audit*: walk the room counting outlets the week before the event.
- **WiFi.** The single most-blamed failure mode. Test the WiFi *with a 30-device load* at least one week out; if the campus IT can give you a *named contact* on event day, get that contact's mobile number; if the WiFi fails, the event continues — but only if the team has *thought about it*.
- **AV.** Projector, screen, microphone, speakers. The named *AV check at venue walkthrough, 1 week out*. Bring backup HDMI and USB-C adapters; the event will need them.
- **Named gate.** *The venue contract is signed, the insurance requirement is named, the access window is named.* Access window = when can the team enter the venue (often 1–2 hours before doors open) and when must the team be out (often 1–2 hours after closing). Named *teardown window* is what you actually have.

### Track C — Capacity

- **First-event default.** 30–60 attendees. Smaller events are easier to run; quality of experience is higher; sponsor expectations are calibrated to the size. The cohort retros are unambiguous: *do not run a 200-person first event*.
- **Capacity is decided early.** It feeds the budget (food, swag, prizes), the venue (square footage, AV), and the sponsorship pitch (audience size).
- **Application versus open-registration.** Application-based admission (with a 60-80% acceptance rate) is more sustainable for first events; open registration tends to over-attract and under-deliver. The named *no-show rate* for free hackathons is 25–35 percent; plan capacity at +20 percent over your seated target.
- **Named gate.** *The capacity is decided and the application opens 4–6 weeks before the event.*

### Track D — Theme

- **Optional but valuable.** A named theme (civic tech, climate, accessibility, ed-tech, health) gives the event a *recruitable narrative*, lets sponsors self-select, and helps attendees scope their projects.
- **Open-theme alternative.** Many strong first events run open-theme. The trade-off: easier to attract attendees, harder to recruit thematic sponsors and thematic judges.
- **Named gate.** *The theme is decided 6 weeks out, when the application opens; changing the theme inside 4 weeks confuses applicants.*

### Track E — Prize pool

Covered in Lecture 3 §3. Decide *named structure* (flat-prize / tracks-and-overall / sponsor-prizes-plus-overall) by week 6 of the runway.

### Track F — Judging format

Covered in Lecture 3 §4. Decide *table-walk versus panel* by week 6 of the runway.

## 3. MLH membership — Local, Member, Premier

Major League Hacking is the dominant student-hackathon coordinator in North America. Affiliating with MLH is *not required* but is *commonly worth it* for first events. The membership tiers as of 2025–2026:

### Tier 1 — Local

- **What it is.** A lightweight affiliation. The event is listed on the MLH event calendar; the event can use the MLH brand in marketing; the event gets access to the public organizer guide and the public Slack.
- **What it does *not* include.** Hardware lab. Insurance via MLH. Direct sponsor introductions. Branded swag.
- **Cost.** Free or near-free as of 2025–2026; verify on `organize.mlh.io/` before scoping the budget.
- **When to use.** First-time student events that are *bootstrapping*; events where the venue and university handle the insurance question; events with a small (sub-100) headcount and a tight budget.

### Tier 2 — Member

- **What it is.** The flagship affiliation. Includes the hardware lab (shipped to the event), branded swag (stickers, sometimes shirts), insurance coverage through MLH's group policy (verify current coverage limits with MLH directly), warm sponsor introductions through MLH's sponsor pipeline, an MLH-provided event coach (a named person who has helped run similar events).
- **What it does *not* include.** Cash sponsorship. MLH does not pay your event; it routes sponsors.
- **Cost.** A membership fee plus a per-event commitment (verify on `organize.mlh.io/` before scoping the budget; the fee structure has changed historically).
- **When to use.** Medium-to-large events (100+ attendees) where the *hardware lab* and the *insurance* are independently worth the fee. The named C4 break-even is around 80 attendees; below that, the Local tier is usually the right call.

### Tier 3 — Premier

- **What it is.** The flagship-flagship affiliation. Includes everything Member includes, plus dedicated MLH staff support, priority placement in the MLH sponsor pipeline, and access to the MLH season calendar (MLH coordinates dates to avoid event overlap among Premier events).
- **When to use.** Returning, established events. The C4 voice is *Premier is not a first-event tier*; ramp Local → Member → Premier across the first three years.

### Application timeline

- **8–12 weeks before event:** Submit the MLH membership application. The MLH team reviews; expect a call or video meeting; expect to be asked about the venue, the code of conduct, the dates, the team. The MLH review is a *vetting*, not a rubber-stamp.
- **6–8 weeks before event:** MLH confirms membership tier. Hardware lab shipping logistics start. Swag order placed.
- **4–6 weeks before event:** MLH coach (if Member tier) starts running periodic check-ins. Sponsor pipeline introductions begin.
- **2 weeks before event:** Hardware lab ships. Swag arrives. Final pre-event check-in.
- **Day-of:** MLH coach is reachable (often on-site for Premier events; on-call for Member events).

### The C4 voice on MLH

For most first-event student teams, **Local tier is the right starting point**. Member tier is the right *second*-event tier, after the first event has proven the team can run an event end-to-end. Premier is the right *third or fourth* event tier. Skipping tiers is possible but expensive (in fee, in commitment, in expectations).

## 4. Insurance — three options

Insurance is the conversation that *kills more first-event hackathons than any other gate*. The named C4 voice on this: *do not run a hackathon without an insurance answer in writing*. The three options:

### Option A — University-provided

- **What it is.** The event is run under the university's umbrella insurance, as an official student-org activity, on a university venue, with a university advisor on record.
- **Who decides.** The campus *Student Activities office* or the campus *Risk Management office*. The named *who-signs* question is answered by the advisor; if there is no faculty/staff advisor on record, the event likely cannot be insured under the university umbrella.
- **Coverage scope.** Usually general liability for the venue, sometimes for the attendees. Varies wildly by university. Get the coverage description *in writing* from the office.
- **Cost.** Free to the event, paid by the university (via the student activity fees or a campus operating budget). The cost to the *event team* is the time spent in the application and approval process — typically 2–4 weeks of back-and-forth.
- **When it works.** For events of any size, hosted on campus, run by registered student orgs, with a faculty/staff advisor. This is the *default first-event path*; if it works, take it.

### Option B — MLH-provided (Member tier and up)

- **What it is.** The event is covered by MLH's group event liability policy. Coverage limits and exact terms change year-to-year; verify with MLH directly during the membership application.
- **When it works.** When the university *will not* underwrite the event (off-campus venue, no advisor, contested student-org status). The MLH-provided policy fills the gap.
- **Caveat.** The MLH policy supplements but does not replace the venue's own requirements. If the venue requires the event to be named as an *additional insured* on a policy, the team must verify whether MLH's policy can satisfy that requirement — it usually can, but the paperwork is non-trivial.

### Option C — Third-party event policy

- **What it is.** A standalone event-insurance policy from an independent broker. Companies like Eventgenic, Markel, and various local brokers underwrite one-off event policies.
- **When it works.** When neither the university nor MLH can or will cover the event. Last-resort path for off-campus, non-MLH-affiliated, no-faculty-advisor events.
- **Cost.** $200–$800 for a small one-day event in the US in 2026 dollars, varying widely by location, attendee count, and coverage limits. Get *three quotes* from three brokers; the price range is wide.
- **Named gate.** *The team buys insurance four weeks out, not the week before; the underwriter needs a week to issue the certificate.*

### The named *minimum coverage*

For a student-run campus hackathon: general liability with at least $1,000,000 per occurrence and $2,000,000 aggregate is the *floor* most venues will require to list the event on the venue calendar. The team does not buy more coverage than the venue requires; the team verifies what the venue requires in writing *before* shopping for coverage.

### The named *who-signs* question

A single human signs the insurance paperwork on behalf of the event. The named *who-signs* question asks: *who is that human, what is their relationship to the event, and what is their exposure if something goes wrong*. For university-provided coverage, the signer is a campus official; the team is not personally exposed. For MLH-provided coverage, MLH signs; the team is not personally exposed but is responsible to MLH for compliance with the conduct expectations. For third-party coverage, one of the organisers signs personally; *that organiser carries personal exposure*. The C4 voice on this: a first-event team should not have a single organiser personally signing a policy; either the university or MLH should be the signer.

## 5. Food and dietary

Food is the named *cheapest line item to get wrong*. Under-ordering creates resentment; over-ordering creates waste; mis-labelling creates allergy incidents; skipping the late-night refuel creates fatigue-driven drop-outs.

### Per-attendee budget

- **One-day campus event (8–10 hours, 2 meals: breakfast + lunch + snacks).** $15–$25 per attendee, total food line in the budget.
- **24-hour event (4 meals: breakfast + lunch + dinner + midnight-snack + breakfast again).** $35–$50 per attendee, total food line.
- **Inflation note.** The numbers above are in *2026 US dollars*; recalibrate every two years against local pricing. The named *take-rate* (percent of attendees who actually eat each meal) hovers around 80–90 percent for breakfast and lunch, 70–80 percent for dinner, 50–70 percent for midnight snacks.

### Meal cadence for a one-day event

- **8:30am — Doors open + breakfast (grab-and-go: bagels, fruit, coffee, granola bars).**
- **12:30pm — Lunch (catered, with dietary options labeled).**
- **3:30pm — Afternoon snacks (chips, fruit, candy, more coffee).**
- **6:30pm — Light dinner if the event runs past 7pm (pizza is the default; cheaper, fewer dietary surprises than a sit-down meal).**

### Meal cadence for a 24-hour event

- **Day 1 — 10am breakfast, 1pm lunch, 4pm snacks, 7pm dinner.**
- **Overnight — 11pm snacks (energy bars, fruit, caffeine), 2am snacks (more pizza, granola bars).**
- **Day 2 — 8am breakfast, 12pm lunch, then submissions close around 1pm.**

### The dietary checklist

The named C4 dietary checklist — *every event must cover all six*:

1. **Vegetarian.** Default 25–35 percent of attendees. Always offer a labeled vegetarian option at every meal.
2. **Vegan.** Default 5–10 percent of attendees. Always offer at least one labeled vegan option at every full meal (lunch and dinner); snacks can rely on naturally vegan items (fruit, chips, hummus).
3. **Gluten-free.** Default 5–10 percent of attendees. Always offer a labeled gluten-free option at lunch and dinner; for breakfast, fruit and yogurt are naturally GF.
4. **Halal.** Required when the campus has Muslim student orgs participating; ask in the application. Many catering providers can offer halal-certified meat; many cannot. If they cannot, the named workaround is vegetarian as a halal-safe default.
5. **Kosher.** Required when the campus has Jewish student orgs participating; ask in the application. Kosher catering is harder and more expensive; the named workaround is *pre-packaged kosher meals* delivered separately.
6. **Nut allergies.** Always label nut-containing items prominently. Never assume "no nuts" — ask the caterer about cross-contamination on the line; for a severe-allergy attendee, plan a separately-sourced meal.

### The labeling rule

Every dish, at every meal, labeled with: dish name, ingredient flags (V = vegetarian, VG = vegan, GF = gluten-free, contains-nuts, contains-dairy). Printed cards, taped to the serving table. Unlabeled food is the named *highest-risk* food-line failure mode; allergic attendees cannot eat what is not labeled, and they will not ask.

### The late-night refuel rule

24-hour events without an 11pm and a 2am refuel see *uneven drop-off*: the half of the team that planned for it stays sharp; the half that did not, crashes. The refuel is cheap (~$2–4 per attendee). The named *do not skip* rule is hard-coded into the C4 voice.

### The named food-fail modes

- **Under-ordering.** Order for 110% of confirmed RSVPs, not 100%. The named *over-order minimum*.
- **No labels.** See above.
- **No late-night refuel.** See above.
- **Caterer cancellation.** Always have a *named backup caterer* (or a named *pizza place that delivers on short notice*). The lead organiser carries the backup phone number.
- **Cold food when warmth was promised.** The named *temperature audit*: a logistics organiser checks the food at delivery; cold pizza is not always wrong, but cold soup is.

## 6. The code of conduct

Every event needs a published code of conduct. *Published* means linked from the event website, printed at the registration desk, and recited at the opening ceremony.

### Pick one — adapt — attribute

The C4 voice is *do not write a code of conduct from scratch*. The named adoptable codes:

- **MLH Code of Conduct.** Published at `mlh.io/code-of-conduct`. Designed for hackathons. Permissively re-usable with attribution.
- **Berlin Code of Conduct.** Published at `berlincodeofconduct.org`. More abstract, easier to adapt. Stronger on the *report-and-response* chain.
- **Contributor Covenant.** Open-source-project-flavored; usable for events with adaptation.

Pick one; adapt with one paragraph that names the event, the venue, and the in-person scope; attribute the source.

### The named incident response chain

Every code of conduct is accompanied by an *incident response chain*. The four named links:

1. **Report channel.** A named email or named phone number that an attendee can use to report an incident. Published at the registration desk and on the event website. The C4 voice: *do not rely on an anonymous form alone*; named humans receive named reports.
2. **On-call organiser.** One named organiser per shift, reachable by phone. The on-call wears a visible identifier (badge color, lanyard, sash). The on-call rotates every 4 hours during a 24-hour event; in a one-day event, two on-calls split the day.
3. **Escalation.** The on-call escalates to the faculty/staff advisor for serious incidents; the advisor escalates to the campus Title IX / Student Conduct office if applicable. The escalation chain is documented; no organiser is asked to triage an incident alone.
4. **Post-incident debrief.** Within 48 hours of the event closing, the organising team meets to debrief any incident that was reported. The named *do not skip* rule: even a single low-severity report gets a debrief.

### The opening-ceremony recitation

The code of conduct is *recited* (not just *linked*) at the opening ceremony. A named organiser reads the one-paragraph summary, names the report channel, points to the on-call organiser, and confirms that the full code of conduct is on the event website. The recitation is 60–90 seconds. It is not optional. Recitation is the public *signal* that the event takes the code seriously; absence of recitation is the public *signal* that it does not.

## 7. The C4 voice on the org-side

The attendee side (Lecture 1) is *individual practice*. The org-side (this lecture) is *structural practice*. The two skills are related but not identical. Strong attendees often make poor first-time organisers because they have not done the work above; the work above is *operational, contractual, and bureaucratic* — the antithesis of the hacker mindset that wins attendee tracks.

A line that re-surfaces in C4 alumni interviews: *"I thought running a hackathon would be like attending one with more responsibility. It was nothing like that. The first 80% of the runway was a paperwork job — venue contract, insurance certificate, sponsor MOU, code-of-conduct adoption, MLH application. The hacking was the easy part on day-of."*

The capstone (mini-project) earns its 7+ hours of work because it walks every named gate in this lecture and the next. By the end of Week 10, the learner has a *paperwork-ready plan* for an event — not a hand-wave.

## 8. Named org-side failure modes

Each failure mode below has a named *fix*. The fixes are cheap in week 8 of the runway and expensive in week 2.

- **Failure mode A — venue verbal hold.** The team has a phone call from the venue contact saying "October 18 is yours" and no written confirmation. The venue books a higher-priority event the week of, displacing the hackathon. Fix: a written hold by week 7 of the runway, at the latest. The fix is *one email* asking for confirmation in writing.
- **Failure mode B — three-organiser team becoming two.** One co-organiser drops out mid-runway (job change, course load, family commitment). The remaining two are below the named three-organiser floor. Fix: recruit one *alternate* organiser at week 8 who shadows the team and steps in if any of the three drops. The alternate is named in the kickoff doc.
- **Failure mode C — insurance answered the week of.** The team assumes the university umbrella covers the event without asking; the week of, the campus events office discovers the event is not registered. Fix: the insurance question is *answered in writing* by week 7. The named *who-signs* question is named.
- **Failure mode D — sponsor commitments verbal.** A sponsor says yes on a call but does not sign the MOU. Two weeks before the event, the sponsor pulls out, citing internal budget changes. The team has counted on the funds. Fix: every sponsor commitment is in writing (MOU + deposit) by week 4 of the runway. Verbal-only sponsor counts are removed from the budget.
- **Failure mode E — code of conduct deferred.** The team plans to publish the code of conduct "closer to the event." A week before the event, the code is not on the website; the registration page does not link to it; the opening-ceremony recital is improvised. Fix: the code of conduct is adapted and published by week 6 of the runway, before applications open.
- **Failure mode F — food ordered late.** The team books the caterer the week of, assuming most caterers can accommodate a 50-person order on three days' notice. Some can; many cannot, especially with the dietary checklist. Fix: caterer contract signed by week 3 of the runway, with backup caterer phone number on file.
- **Failure mode G — MLH application past the window.** The team intends to apply for MLH Member tier but waits until week 4 of the runway. The MLH review cycle is 6–8 weeks; by the time the application is reviewed, the event has passed. Fix: MLH application submitted by week 8 of the runway (or the Local tier is chosen instead, which has a shorter review cycle).

## 9. The org-side as a portfolio item

The C4 voice on the *resume placement* of organising work is that the event-organising role is *Experience-section material*, not *Projects-section material*. The distinction matters:

- **Projects section.** Where the hackathon's *built submission* lives. Verb-first bullets, named outcomes, links to repo and demo.
- **Experience section.** Where the *organiser role* lives. Title is *"Organising Lead, [Event Name] [Year]"* or *"Co-Organiser, [Event Name] [Year]"*. Date range covers the runway plus the event week. Description is 2 paragraphs:
  - Paragraph 1 — what the event was (size, theme, venue, named outcomes — attendee count, sponsor count, projects-submitted count, judging panel size).
  - Paragraph 2 — what the organiser personally owned (the named track from the team — sponsorship, logistics, programming) and what shipped under their ownership (named numbers — *$X raised in sponsorship*, *N judges recruited*, *attendee survey response rate*, *budget vs. actual variance*).

The Experience-section placement signals *operational ownership*, which is a different signal from *technical contribution*. Recruiters for *program manager*, *technical program manager*, *engineering manager*, *founder*, and *developer-relations* roles weight Experience-section organising experience heavily. The signal is durable; one well-described organising role on a LinkedIn profile earns inbound recruiter messages for years.

A line that re-surfaces in C4 alumni interviews: *"I had four hackathons listed under Projects when a recruiter messaged me about a developer-relations role. The reason they reached out was the one organising line I'd added to Experience the year before. The Projects were table stakes; the Experience line was the conversation starter."*

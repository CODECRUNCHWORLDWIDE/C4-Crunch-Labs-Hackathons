Every resource on this page is **free** and **publicly accessible**. No paywalled books, no signups beyond the free tier. If a link breaks, please open an issue.

## Planning poker and pointing tools

- **PlanITPoker (free, no signup).** Browser-based planning-poker rooms for remote teams; the free tier is enough for any hackathon team: <https://www.planitpoker.com/>
- **Scrum Poker Online (free).** Simpler interface, also free, also no signup required: <https://scrumpoker.online/>
- **Index cards or sticky notes.** A pack of 100 sticky notes is the most cost-effective planning-poker deck on Earth. For in-person hackathons, this beats every browser tool.
- **A printable 1-2-3-5 deck.** Print four copies of a page with the numbers `1 2 3 5 ?` in large type. Hand one set to each teammate. That is the entire toolkit.

C4's default is sticky notes for in-person events and PlanITPoker for remote. Both work. The tool is not the skill; the skill is the discussion that the tool forces.

## On "saying no" without breaking the room

- **Atlassian — How to say "no" at work (Team Playbook).** Practical, short, free; the framing is what we adapt for hackathon scope-creep: <https://www.atlassian.com/team-playbook/plays>
- **The "yes, and… no, because" pattern.** Improv-derived; search any blog for a one-page overview. The structure — acknowledge, then refuse with a reason — is what hackathon teams need.
- **Re-read C4 Week 1 Lecture 2 §"Demo or die".** The discipline of cutting scope to fit a demo is the same discipline of saying no to a teammate's suggestion. The voice is the same.

## On story-pointing under time pressure

- **"Why story points are a relative measure, not a time estimate."** Search Mountain Goat Software for the post; the headline is the value. Points are *size relative to each other*, not hours.
- **"The 1-2-3-5 scale" (C4 default).** A truncated Fibonacci, easier to argue about in 30 seconds than a 1-2-3-5-8-13-21 scale. Lecture 2 §1 explains why.
- **The "three discussion points" rule.** If a card takes more than 30 seconds of discussion to point, the discussion *is* the value, not the number. Slow down on those cards; speed up on the obvious ones.

## On the four ceremony failure modes

Each failure mode below has a one-sentence test. Lecture 1 §5 elaborates.

- **The silent stand-up.** "We just took turns reporting status; nobody asked anyone for help." Test: did at least one teammate ask another a question during stand-up? If no, the stand-up failed.
- **The demo-as-showcase.** "We showed off what we built; nobody named what we are cutting." Test: did the demo end with a named cut decision? If no, the demo failed.
- **The blame retro.** "We talked about who messed up X." Test: were any sentences in the retro phrased "Pat broke Y"? If yes, the retro failed.
- **Points-as-promises.** "We pointed this at 3 so it has to be done in 3 hours." Test: did anyone reference a point estimate as a time commitment? If yes, the pointing failed.

Read each test out loud once. You will recognize at least two of them from past group projects. Naming them is half the cure.

## Glossary cheat sheet

| Term | Plain English |
|------|---------------|
| **Stand-up (daily scrum)** | A 60-second-per-person sync to coordinate work and surface blockers. Not a status report to a manager. |
| **Internal demo (mid-event)** | A 10–15 minute show-and-tell among teammates at hour 12 and hour 24. The goal is to find what to cut, not to applaud. |
| **Retro (retrospective)** | A 30-minute structured reflection on the sprint. Keep / start / stop. Produces three behavior changes per teammate. |
| **Story point** | A relative size estimate for a backlog item. Not hours. Not a promise. Used on the 1-2-3-5 scale at C4. |
| **Planning poker** | A pointing format where everyone reveals their estimate simultaneously, then discusses the outliers. Ten items, ten minutes, no debate without a question. |
| **The "no" script** | Three concrete sentences that cut a scope request without breaking the room. Acknowledge, refuse with a reason, offer a cheaper alternative. |
| **Scope creep** | A scope item that enters the build mid-sprint without going through the cut-list. The single most common cause of hackathon failure. |
| **Team contract** | A one-page written agreement on how *this* team runs stand-ups, decides cuts, points stories, and handles disagreement. The artifact you bring to the next event. |
| **Rotating timekeeper** | The role of cutting people off at 60 seconds during stand-up. Rotates each ceremony so no one becomes the "stand-up boss." |
| **Blameless voice** | "The sprint board didn't catch the rate limit" — not "Pat broke the rate limit." Carries over from Week 3 unchanged. |

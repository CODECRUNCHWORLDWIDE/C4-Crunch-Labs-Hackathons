# Week 4 — Quiz

Ten multiple-choice questions. Take it with your lecture notes closed. Aim for 9/10 before moving to Week 5. Answer key at the bottom — do not peek.

---

**Q1.** A ceremony (stand-up, demo, or retro) that does not produce a decision is best described as:

- A) A team-building moment; no decision is required.
- B) A failure — the ceremony exists to produce a decision and skipped its job.
- C) Acceptable as long as everyone enjoyed it.
- D) A success if it stayed under the time cap.

---

**Q2.** The 60-second team stand-up has four questions. Which is the team-level question asked once at the end?

- A) "What did I ship in the last 6 hours?"
- B) "What is the next 6 hours of work?"
- C) "What is in my way?"
- D) "What do we cut to still ship?"

---

**Q3.** The rotating timekeeper role exists to:

- A) Make one person the "stand-up boss" who enforces discipline.
- B) Concentrate the social cost of cutting people off and spread it evenly across the team.
- C) Reduce the number of stand-ups per event.
- D) Replace the team lead at every ceremony.

---

**Q4.** At hour 24 of a 36-hour event, the internal demo uses a "stranger" test. The stranger's job is to:

- A) Critique the team's code quality and architecture choices.
- B) Open the live URL with no setup, narrate aloud what they see, and try to perform the MUST verb — while teammates listen silently.
- C) Replace one teammate who is underperforming.
- D) Coach the team through the demo path until it works.

---

**Q5.** On the C4 default story-pointing scale, what is the largest number on a card?

- A) 13.
- B) 8.
- C) 5 — anything bigger should be split or pointed as "?".
- D) 21.

---

**Q6.** A teammate says, "I'll have the auth done in 3 hours — it's 3 points." This is an example of:

- A) Correct usage of story points.
- B) Points-as-promises — the failure mode where points are read as time commitments instead of relative size.
- C) Acceptable language as long as the estimate is accurate.
- D) The "yes" variant of saying no.

---

**Q7.** The three-sentence "no" script's middle sentence — "Refuse with a reason" — should reference:

- A) Your personal preference about the feature.
- B) The team contract's WON'T list and the one-sentence reason.
- C) The team lead's opinion.
- D) A vague rejection — "I just don't think we should."

---

**Q8.** A teammate at hour 12 proposes adding a leaderboard. The team agrees it sounds great. The correct next step is:

- A) Start building it immediately; it's a good idea.
- B) Add it to MUST without further discussion.
- C) Apply the "yes, *with an equal-points cut*" rule — point the new card, cut a same-points card from MUST, re-confirm at the next stand-up.
- D) Postpone the discussion until hour 30.

---

**Q9.** The 30-minute team retro at hour 36 begins with:

- A) Round-robin status reports from each teammate.
- B) The facilitator restating the goal (3 behaviors per teammate by hour 36:30), followed by 8 minutes of *silent writing* before any discussion.
- C) The team lead summarizing the event.
- D) A 30-minute open-ended conversation about feelings.

---

**Q10.** Which is the **most accurate** statement about the team contract (this week's mini-project)?

- A) It is optional; teams that gel naturally do not need one.
- B) It is filled in at hour 12 of the event, once teammates have met each other.
- C) It is a one-page written agreement — committed before hour 0 — that names how the team runs stand-ups, decides cuts, points stories, and handles disagreement; without it, every ceremony is re-negotiated at every ceremony.
- D) It replaces all Agile ceremonies; teams with a strong contract do not need stand-ups.

---

## Answer key

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

1. **B** — A ceremony exists to produce a decision. No decision = failure. Run the next one tighter. (Lecture 1 §1.1)
2. **D** — "What do we cut to still ship?" is the team-level Q4. Q1–Q3 are per-teammate. (Lecture 1 §2.1)
3. **B** — The rotating timekeeper spreads the social cost of cutting people off; it is an alarm clock with a name, not a manager. (Lecture 1 §2.2)
4. **B** — The stranger opens the URL with no setup, narrates aloud, and teammates listen silently. The friction the stranger surfaces is the demo's whole value. (Lecture 1 §3.2)
5. **C** — 5 is the largest. Anything bigger gets split or pointed as "?". No 8, no 13. (Lecture 2 §1.2)
6. **B** — Points-as-promises. Points are relative size, not time. The fix is language discipline. (Lecture 2 §2.2)
7. **B** — The contract's WON'T list and the one-sentence reason. The contract is the third party that holds the line. (Lecture 2 §3.2)
8. **C** — The "yes, *with an equal-points cut*" rule. Added scope produces an equal-points cut from MUST. No exceptions. (Lecture 2 §3.5)
9. **B** — The facilitator restates the goal, then 8 minutes of silent writing. Silent writing surfaces every voice; talking-first retros are dominated by the loudest voice. (Lecture 1 §4.1)
10. **C** — Committed before hour 0, one page, names how the team runs ceremonies and disagreements. Without it, every ceremony is re-negotiated. (Lecture 1 §9 and the mini-project README)

</details>
